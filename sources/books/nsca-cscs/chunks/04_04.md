Adrenal Gland, Cortisol, and Its Role in
Resistance Exercise
The adrenal gland is integr al to the body’s adaptation to stress,
particularly during and after exercise. Structur ally, it consists of two
main compone nts: the medul la and the cortex, each responding to
stress in distinct ways. The adrenal medul la, the inner core of the
gland, is directly stimulated by the autonomic nervous system during
acute stress. It rapidly secretes catecholamines such as epinephrine
and norepinephrine, alongside dopamine and specific enkephal in-
containing polypeptides like peptide F, which are implicated in
immediate stress responses and immune enhancement (129). The
adrenal cortex is stimulated by adrenocorticotr opic hormone (ACTH)
from the anterior pituitary gland to produce and release cortisol.
Cortisol has critical roles in modulating inflammation and managing
metabol ic responses to stress. As such, cortisol is regulated on a
systemic level by the hypoth alamic -pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and
locally within tissues by enzymes such as 11β-hydroxy- steroid
dehydrogenase (11β-HSD) to maintain glucocorticoid balance (25,
27, 99). Cortisol is crucial for sustaining energy during prolonged
stress by facilitating glucose availability through selectiv e glucose
sparing and mobilization, gluconeogenesis, and fat and protein
catabol ism. Howev er, prolonged elevation of cortisol levels—if not
returned to baseline within 24 hours—can adversely affect the body,
because cortis ol in this context is consider ed catabol ic and anti-
anabol ic. Sustained high cortisol can impair immune function, affect
fat metabol ism and deposi tion, reduce protein synthesis, increase
protein degradation, and interfere with anabol ic signal ing pathways,
thereby promoting a catabol ic state that can undermine muscle
repair and growth (75, 87, 89, 92, 129). Interference in anabol ic
signal ing is partly due to the cortisol significantly contributing to
immune suppr ession observ ed after intense exercise stress. Cortisol
is crucial in “inactiv ating” immune cell functions during recovery

Cortisol is critical in energy balance, immune regulation, and

training, particularly with high intensi ty or volume involving large
muscle masses. The acute hormonal response prepares the body for
future physical demands and aids r ecovery.
The resistance exercise cortisol response differs some between
men and women. Men gener ally experience higher increases in
cortisol from certain types of training protocols, such as high-volume
workouts, whereas women rapidly adjust their receptor sensitivity to
hormones after exercise. Such data indicate a sex-speci fic adaptation
to training stress (133). Emerging resear ch shows that resistance
exercise can affect glucocorticoid receptor concentr ations differently
across sexes, indicating potential sex differences in hormonal
response to training. These findings underscor e the complex
interaction between cortisol and its receptors in regulating muscle
adaptation and r ecovery (31).

Catecholamines and Their Role in
Resistance Exercise and Training
Catecholamines, primari ly epinephrine but also including
norepinephrine and dopamine, are secreted by the adrenal medul la
and play a crucial role in enhancing strength and power during
resistance training. These hormones facilitate the fight-or-flight
response by acting as central motor stimulators and peripher al
vascular dilators, enhancing enzyme activity and calcium release in
muscles (89). Notably , higher catecholamine release before and
during intense resistance sessions has been linked to sustained force
output, empha sizing their importance in maintaining performance
(32).
Physiological Functions of Catecholamines
Catecholamines enhance resistance training outcomes by doing the
following:
Increasing force production through central mechanisms and
boosting metabol ic enzyme activi ty
Accelerating muscle contr action r ates
Raising blood pr essure
Enhancing energy availability and muscle blood flow through
vasodilation
Augmenting the secretion rates of other anabol ic hormones

typical in bodybui lding—char acteriz ed by increased levels of
norepinephrine, epinephrine,  and dopamine during the session and
brief recovery periods—i llustrates catecholamines’ role in meeting
the physical demands of such protocols (86). Epinephrine levels also

Training Adaptations and Long-Term
Impacts
Heavy resistance training enhances an athlete’ s capaci ty to secrete
more epinephrine during maximal efforts (87). Consiste nt training
may decrease epinephrine responses to single sessions, such as a
bench press workout (44). This adaptation suggests that regular
training can modulate catecholamine responses, reducing their surge
during familiar tasks to maintain a more balanced hormonal
environment conduciv e to ongoing strength gains and recovery.
Understanding the role of catecholamines in resistance training helps
in designing programs that optimiz e hormonal responses to enhance
strength, hypertrophy, and overall athletic performance.
Catecholamines’ ability to interact with other hormones underscor es
their pivotal position in the endocrine response to resistance
exercise, highlighting the importance of managing exercise intensi ty,
volume, and r ecovery to maximiz e beneficial hormonal inter actions.
Training protocols must be varied to allow the adrenal
gland to engage in recovery processes and to prevent
the secondary responses of cortisol, which can have
negative effects on the immune system and protein
structures.

Other Hormonal Considerations in
Resistance Training
A variety of hormones contribute to normal bodily functions and
adaptiv ely respond to resistance training, including insulin, thyroid
hormones, and beta-endorp hin. These hormones play roles in
growth, repair, pain relief, and managing exercise stress, though
resear ch on their specific adaptations to resistance training is limited
(15, 26, 27, 28, 51, 64, 71, 75, 82, 98). For healthy individuals,
significant chronic changes in the resting concentr ations of insulin
and thyroid hormones are gener ally not observ ed due to tight
homeostatic control. Howev er, resistance training has been noted to
improve insulin sensitivity, possibly reflecting only the acute effects
of recent ex ercise sessions (10).
Thyroid Hormones
Thyroid hormo nes, particula rly triiodoth yronine (T3) and thyroxine
(T4), play a significant role in both acute and chronic responses to
resistance exercise and training. Immediately following resistance
exercise, T3 levels gener ally remain unchanged, while T4 levels
might spike briefly and then decrease during subsequent rest
periods (65, 107). This acute response suggests a tempor ary
adjustment to meet the immediate metabol ic demands of ex ercise.
In the context of long-term resistance training, transient
reductions in T3 and T4 levels can occur, which typical ly normal ize
after several months (3, 116). These fluctuations indicate an initial
adaptation phase in which the thyroid axis adjusts to the increased
demands of r egular r esistance ex ercise.
Thyroid hormones are crucial for metabol ic control and augment
other hormonal responses, underscoring their importance in
physiological adaptations to training. They support the energy
demands of resistance training by regulating protein synthesis and
mitochondrial activity, contributing to muscle repair and growth.

Specifically, T3 enhances protein synthesis by increasing the
transcription of genes involved in muscle hypertr ophy and repair.
Additional ly, T4’s conversion to T3 in peripher al tissues ensur es a
steady supply of activ e hormones to meet ongoing metabol ic needs.
Thyroid hormones also help regulate lipolysis and glucose
metabol ism, which are essential for providing the necessary energy
substr ates during and after resistance exercise. By modulating these
processes, thyroid hormones help maintain energy balance and
facilitate recovery. Furthermor e, thyroid hormones interact with
other endocrine responses during resistance training. For example,
they can enhance the action of GH and IGF-1, both of which are
vital for muscle growth and repair. This syner gistic effect amplifies
the overall anabol ic environment, promoting more effective muscle
adaptation and strength gains. Thus, thyroid hormones are integr al
to immediate and long-term resistance exercise adaptat ions. They
ensur e metabol ic homeostasis, support energy production, enhance
protein synthesis, and interact with other hormones to facilitate
muscle growth and repair. Understanding their role highlights the
complex hormonal interpl ay required for effective training
adaptations and o verall metabol ic regulation.
Insulin
The insulin response to resistance exercise and training is crucial to
metabol ic regulation and muscle adaptation. Physiological ly,
resistance exercise induces an acute increase in insulin sensitivity,
which enhances glucose uptak e into skeletal muscle, facilitating
glycogen synth esis and promoting anabol ic processes essential for
muscle repair and growth (36, 51). This response is attributed to
increased GLUT4 expression on the cell surface, driven by both
contraction-mediated and insulin-mediated pathways (101). Regular
resistance training amplifies this effect, improving overall short- and
long-term insulin sensitivity. Such data are particularly significant for
metabol ic health, because enhanced insulin sensitivity associated
with regular resistance training can contribute to better glycemic

Conclusion
The endocrine system’ s interaction with the nervous, immune, and
musculosk eletal systems forms a complex network crucial for
optimizing health and athletic performance. Hormones coordinate a
wide range of cellular activities, influencing muscle synthesis,
recovery, and overall physiological well-being. The dynamic balance
between anabol ic hormones, which promote muscle growth, and
catabol ic hormones, respons ible for muscle breakdown and energy
mobilization is vi tal for responding ef fectively to r esistance tr aining.
Strength and condi tionin g professionals need a deep
understanding of the endocrine system’ s functions to design
effective training programs that maximiz e performance and minimiz e
injury risks. This knowledge enables the manipulation of hormonal
responses to both acute exercise and chronic training adaptations,
enhancing recovery and preventing overtraining. The subtle interpla y
among various bodily systems and hormones has significant
implications beyond athletic performance, affecting condi tions such
as metabol ic syndr ome, insulin resistance, and cardiovascular health.
By leveraging insights from the endocrine system, coaches and
athletes can achiev e peak performance and support long-term
health.
This overview highlights the sophisticated yet orderly system that
mediates the body’s respons es to resistance exercise, emphasizing
the critical role of the endocr ine system in sport science and overall
health manage ment. Unders tanding these complex interactions is
fundamental for strength and condi tioning professionals and athletes
aiming to optimiz e training outcomes and ensur e sustained athletic
success and wel l-being.

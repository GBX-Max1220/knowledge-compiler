Testing the 1RM
To assign training loads based on a percentage of the 1RM, the strength and
condi tioning professional must first determine the athlete’ s 1RM. This method of
assessment is typical ly reserv ed for resistance-tr ained athletes who are classified
as intermediat e or advanced and have exercise techniq ue experience in the
exercises being tested; however, 1RM testing can be safely performed with
youth (19), adolescents (49), inexperienced adults (24), and older adults (127).
Individuals who are untrained, inexperienced, injured, or medical ly supervised
may not be appropriate participants for 1RM testing. One-r epetition maximum
testing requires an adequate  training status and lifting experience, because the
assessment of maximal strength places significant stress on the involved
muscles, connectiv e tissues, and joints, although this has been shown to be
appropriate and reliable in youth and collegiate athletes  with approximately 6
months’ experience in the power clean and back squat (24, 49). Howev er, it may
be more appropriate to complete a 3RM test instead of a maximal 1RM test
because ignori ng an athlete’ s training status and exercise technique experience
diminishes the saf ety and ac curacy of 1RM test r esults.
When selectin g exercises for 1RM testing, the strength and condi tioning
professional should choose core exercises, because the large muscle groups and
multiple joints are better able to handle the heavy loads. Despi te this guidel ine,
an exercise should not be selected for 1RM testing if it cannot provide valid and
reliable data (i.e., does not accurately and consistently assess maximal muscular
strength). For instance, the large upper back musculatur e and multiple joints
involved in the bent-over row exercise can probably tolerate the loads for a 1RM
test, but maintaining a correct body position throughout testing would be
extremely difficult. The weaker stabilizing muscles of the lower back might

become very fatigued after several testing sets, resulting in a loss of proper
exercise technique and an in valid test.
A variety of procedur es can be used to accurately determine a 1RM; one
method is described in figure 18.8. Despite an orderly testing sequence,
variations in training status and exercise type will affect the absolute load
increases in sequential testing sets. For example, the gradual load increase for
1RM attempts for an athlete who can back squat 495 pounds (225 kg) may be
20 to 30 pounds (9-14 kg) per testing set. For a weaker athlete with a back
squat 1RM of 100 pounds (45 kg), a 20- or 30-pound testing load increment is
too aggressive and is not precise enough to yield an accurate 1RM value. To
improve the appropriateness and accuracy of the sequen tial testing sets, figure
18.8 also includes relative percentages that can be used instead of the absolute
load adjustments.
Estimating a 1RM
When maximal strength testing is not warranted, testing with a 3RM or 5RM
load (and then estimating or predicting the 1RM) can be a suitable secondary
option. This approach is appropriate for nearly all athletes, provided they can
demonstr ate the proper technique in the exercise tested. Core and assistance
exercises can be selected for RM testing, but excessiv e warm-up and testing sets
may fatigue the athlete and compr omise the accuracy of the test. It should be
noted, howev er, that the level of accuracy of predicting a 1RM from other xRM
tests becomes less accurate (i.e., commonly resulting in overestimations of the
1RM) as the number of repetitions completed increases and may also be
affected by the type of training with which the athlete is familiar (134).
Additional ly, power exercises do not lend themselv es well to xRM testing above 5
repetitions for repeated testing sets because technique can deterior ate rapidly
(71). Lower (and more accurate) xRM determinations using heavier loads can be
made once the athlete has suf ficient tr aining and technique experience.
The protocol for multiple RM testing is similar to that for 1RM testing, but
each set requires multiple repetitions, not one. After the completion of warm-up
sets, the athlete’s sequential  load changes for the 10RM  test are smaller than
those listed in figure 18.8 (approximately one-hal f). Continue the process of
testing until a load allowing the selected number of repetitions is determined. An
experienced strength and conditioning professional will be able to adjust the
loads so that the mul tiple RM can be measur ed wi thin 3 to 5 testing sets.

xRM Testing Based on Goal Repetitions
A third option for determining training loads requires the strength and
condi tioning professional to first decide on the number of repetitions (i.e., the
goal repetitions ) the athlete will perform in the actual program for the
exercise being tested. For example, if the strength and condi tioning professional
decides that the athlete should perform 6 repetitions for the bench press
exercise in the training program, the xRM testing protocol should have the
athlete perform the exercise with a load that will result in 6 repetitions (6RM).
Core and assistance exercises can be selected for xRM testing, but as previously
mentioned, high-repetition testing sets can create significant fatigue and may
compr omise the accuracy of the tested xRM. This effect seems to be more
problematic for exercises that involve multiple joints and large muscle areas due
to their high metabol ic demand (138, 185). Further , xRM testing (and
subsequent load assignments) for assistance exercises should be at or above an
8RM to minimiz e the isolativ e stress on the involved joint and connectiv e tissue
(2, 46). In other words, even if an athlete is following a muscular strength
training program that involves 2RM loads for the core exercises, the heaviest
load the assistance ex ercises should be assigned is an 8RM.

Rating of Perceived Exertion and Repetitions in
Reserve
Beyond percentages of 1RM, other methods of load prescription are available to
strength and condi tioning professionals. The rating of perceived exertion
(RPE)  provides a subjectiv e measur ement of work based on the perception of
an individual. While the original RPE scale (i.e., 6-20) was based on aerobic
activity (15), a modified CR10 scale (i.e., 0-10) has been examined by
resear chers when monitoring resistance training exercise intensi ty within an
individual set (92, 135) and during entire training sessions (i.e., session RPE)
(37, 107, 181). Similarly, repetitions in reserve (RIR)  provides a subjectiv e
measur e of the perceived number of repetitions that theoretically could be
performed following an exercise set (199). Hackett and colleagues (64, 66)
1231
displa yed very large relationships between estimated RIR and actual RIR and
showed that RIR estimations become more accurate when exercise sets are
performed closer to failure. These findings are supported by additional
resear chers (100, 198); thus, strength and condi tioning professionals must
consider that the relative intensi ty of each exercise set may need to be higher
for this method to be usef ul for load pr escription purposes.
When impleme nting RPE and RIR, it is recommended to prescribe intensi ties
in the form of ranges (e.g., RPE of 7-8 or RIR of 2-3) (176). Although relatively
few studies exist, some have shown that load prescription based on RPE for 8
weeks (73) and RIR for 12 weeks (60) may lead to greater strength
improvements compar ed to using percentages of 1RM. Howev er, further studies
with additional athlete popul ations are needed before concr ete conclusions can
be drawn. RPE and RIR may be best used if combined with an objectiv e method
(e.g., percentage of 1RM) so that the desired loads for a given exercise can be
determined before using RPE/RIR to adjust the load for autoregulation purposes
(176). Professionals should note that the ability to gauge physical exertion may
vary based on athletic experience (4). For example, inexperienced athletes may
not understand what a near-maximal or maximal resistance training effort feels
like. Thus, strength and conditioning professionals should consider limiting load
autonom y until the athletes  can displa y proficiency with the RPE and RIR
methods.
Set-Repetition Best
Original ly proposed by Stone and O’Bryant (162), set-repetition best (SRB)  is
a load prescrip tion method that uses relative intensi ties (i.e., percent ranges)
where the maximum load is estimated based on performances of a given set and
repetition scheme. In other words, SRB determines loads based on percentages
of what was performed in training. Specifically, 5% ranges are prescribed to
allow strength  and condi tioning professionals to observ e the performance of
their athletes and incorpor ate athlete feedback as a means of autoregulation.
Beyond the ability to adjust weekly training prescription, SRB allows specific
training loads to be estima ted when prescribing different set and repetition
schemes (e.g., loads for 3 sets of 5 repetitions should be approximately 15%
less than 3 sets of 2 repetitions) (162, 176). Compar ed to prescribing xRM
percentages based on single sets, SRB is based on multiple sets so that an
athlete’ s training loads are based on relative percentages of the prescribed set-
repetition scheme. Using SRB, any set-repetition scheme can be prescribed and
progressed using relative 5% ranges. For example, 3 sets of 5 repetitions or 3
sets of 10 repetitions can be prescribed with 85% to 90% of the respectiv e
estimated maximal load for 3 sets of 5 or 3 sets of 10, respectiv ely, in week 1 of

a training phase and progress to 90% to 95% the following week. Within a
single week of training, heavy days are typical ly prescribed with loads
approximately 10% to 15% heavier than the lighter days of training (42). Thus,
SRB may account for the fatigue that accumulates across multiple sets during
training. This may allow training loads to be adjusted on a weekly basis without
the need to perform RM testing on a regular basis. Table 18.10 shows SRB

How to Calculate a Traini ng Load  If an athlete’ s training goal is muscular
strength and the tested 1RM in the bench press exercise is 220 pounds (100 kg),
to increase strength, the athlete needs to handle loads of at least 85% of the
1RM (after warm-up) that typical ly allow performance of up to 6 repetitions
within a single set (table 18.11). More specifically, if the strength and
condi tioning professional assigns 4 repetitions for this exercise, the
corresponding load could be approximately 90% of the 1RM (table 18.8), or
approximately 200 pounds (91 kg). Note that the strength and condi tioning
professional should make adjustments to the assign ed loads based on
observ ation of the ease or difficulty an athlete experiences in lifting the load for
the required repetitions. Moreover, it is important to avoid training to failure on a
regular basis because it may not be necessary to maximiz e muscular
hypertr ophy, strength, or power adaptations (36, 63, 137, 191). By minimizing
training to failure, strength  and condi tioning professionals may reduce the
overall training  strain on athletes while still providing  an effective training
stimulus and maximizing perf ormance gains (17 , 18).
Assigning Percentages for Power Training  The force–velocity curve
illustrates that the greater the amount of concentric muscular force gener ated,
the slower the muscle shortening and corresponding movement velocity (and
vice versa). Maximal power , in contrast, may be produced at intermediate
velocities with the lifting of light to moder ate loads in nonbal listic, traditional
resistance training exercises such as the squat or bench press (27, 105, 154,
157). Performing a 1RM involves slower movement velocities; near-maximal
forces are gener ated but with reduced power output (56, 197). Seldom is an
athlete required to demonstr ate a singular , maximal, slow-speed muscular
strength effort in a sport (except in powerl ifting, for example). Most sport
movements are faster and involve higher power outputs than those produced
during a 1RM test (89, 118). This does not mean that an athlete’ s power
capabi lities are unaffected by maximal muscular strength training, howev er.
1238
Because speed- or power -related sport movements often begin from zero or
near-zero velocities, slow-velocity strength gains have direct application to rapid
force developm ent and power production (1, 22). For these reasons, the load
and repetition assignments for power training overlap the guidel ines for strength
training ( table 18.11 ).
Non-weightl ifting, multijoint power exercises (e.g., jump squat, bench press
throw, overhead press throw) and single-joint muscle action data reveal that
peak power is gener ally reached with the lifting of very light loads—f rom body
weight (0%) to 30% of the 1RM (27, 105, 154, 157, 174, 175). Performing some
of these exercises (e.g., bench press throw and overhead press throw) in a
Smith machine , for example, can help to address the safety issues. On the other
end of the load continuum, data from multiple national- and world-class
weightl ifting and powerl ifting championships clearly indicate that power output
increases as the weight lifted decreases from 100% of the 1RM (i.e., the 1RM)
to 90% of the 1RM (56). In fact, for the back squat and deadl ift exercises,
power output for a load at 90% of the 1RM may be twice as high as with the
1RM load due to a large decrease in the time required to complete the exercise
with the lighter load (57). Even for the already “fast” power exercises
(weightl ifting-based movements), there is still a 5% to 10% increase in power
output as the load decreases from the 1RM to 90% of the 1RM (57). Considering
these issues, the most effective and practical application is to assign loads that
are about 75% to 90% of the 1RM for resistance training exercises that can be
heavily loaded such as the snatch and clean and other weightl ifting-deriv ed
movements (27, 56, 105). However, the loading that maximiz es power output in
weightl ifting movements and their derivatives is exercise specific. For example,
the highest power outputs of catching variations (e.g., hang power clean and
power clean) were shown between 60% and 80% 1RM (20, 83, 85, 168), while
pulling derivatives may maxim ize power output at either lighter (i.e., <50% 1RM
of a catching variation) (86, 167, 169, 179, 180) or at much heavier loads (i.e.,
100%-120% 1RM) (23, 25, 111, 112, 115) depending on the velocity (e.g., jump
shrug and hang high pull) or force potential (e.g., countermo vement shrug, mid-
thigh pull, hang pull) of the exercises, respectiv ely. Strength and condi tioning
professionals should also not overlook the use of overhead pressing derivatives
(e.g., push press and jerk) for developing power output (156). Resear chers have
shown that maximal power output during the jerk and back jerk occurred
between 80% and 90% 1RM of the respectiv e exercises in competi tive

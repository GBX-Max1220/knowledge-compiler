Sickle C ell Trait
The sickle cell trait  (SCT ) is an inheri ted condi tion char acteriz ed by
one abnormal hemoglobin gene and one normal hemoglobin gene.
Individuals with SCT may experience compl ications akin to sickle cell
disease under specific circumstances like extreme physical exertion
or low oxygen levels (72). Unlike sickle cell disease , which
necessi tates inheri ting two abnormal genes, SCT carriers usually do
not exhibi t severe symptoms. In situations such as high-altitude
training environments or extreme exertion where oxygen levels are
low, the red blood cells can change to an abnormal shape (sickling)
and begin to clump together , which ultimately may be responsible
for constrictio n of the blood vessels. When sickling and the
associated vascular occlusion occurs, reports of fatigue or tiredness,
pain, swelling of hand and feet, and vision problems may occur (69).
Longer term consequences of sickling include damage to the blood
vessels; a shorter life span of existing red blood cells, leading to
anemia; and, in cases of vascular occlusion, stroke. Evidence
suggests that the SCT is associated with sudden death during
periods of extreme exertion  and is more common among young
African Americ ans (69). A majori ty of deaths attributed to the SCT
have occurred during military training and athletic participation at
the col legiate lev el (69).
Should an individual develop symptoms of sickling, they should
immediately discontinue physical activity and be removed from
extreme temper ature environments. Owing to potential dehydration,
proper hydration protocols should be implemented immed iately (95).
Immediate referral to a medical professional is indicated if acute
symptoms ar e present.

Heat Cramps
Heat cramps are part of a continuum of heat-related injuries or
illnesses marked by dysregulation of the body’s thermor egulation
capaci ty (58). Heat cramps  are muscle spasms or involuntary
sustained contractions of large muscle groups that may occur when
the athlete is exposed to elevated environmental temper atures
during exercise. Heat cramps may occur during or shortly after
exercise. Several factors may contribute to heat cramps, including
dehydration, depleted electr olytes, increased training  load or
duration, and muscular fatigue. Although heat cramps gener ally
occur in hot environments, elevated core body temper ature is not
always present.
When heat cramps are suspected, immediate cessation of activity
and moving the athlete to a cooler environment to lower body
temper ature is a necessi ty. Implementing proper rehydration is
essential as are appropriate cooling measur es. Passive gentle
stretching or massage to affected muscle groups may be beneficial
(58). Heat cramps may be prevented by proper hydration protocols,
use of light-weight clothing when exercising in hot environments,
and appropriate progressive condi tioning in the specific training
environment to al low heat ac climation (58).

Heat Syncope
Heat syncope  occurs when an athlete experiences dizziness or
lightheadedness and faints during or immediately after training (12).
Heat syncope may occur during positional changes or with prolonged
positioning during exercise in hot environments. Risk factors for heat
syncope include training in hot environments without prior heat
acclimation, dehydration, pooling of blood in the venous system, and
low blood pressure (58). When syncope is suspected or fainting
occurs it is important to lay the athlete in a supine position and
elevate the legs above the level of the heart. If fainting has
occurred, monitoring of vital signs and shielding of the body from
direct heat exposur e is recommended. Once appropriate, proper oral
rehydration is necessary . Prevention strategies include proper
acclimation to training in hot environments and ensuring proper
hydration before, during, and after training. If low blood pressure or
frequent syncope occurs despite preventativ e efforts, medical
consul tation is necessary .
Heat Exhaustion
Heat exhaustion  is the inability to effectively exercise in the heat.
Numer ous factors contribute to heat exhaustion including low blood
pressure, dehydration, lack of acclimation to exercise in hot
environments, heavy sweating, and electr olyte depletion  (12, 58).
Symptoms of heat exhaustion include inability to continue exercise,
pallor, heat cramps, weakness, syncope, dizziness, headache,
hyperv entilation, nausea, diarrhea, acute loss of appeti te, decreased
or dark urine output, and elevated core temper ature up to 104.9 °F
(40.5 °C) (12, 58).
If heat exhaustion is recogniz ed, immediately remove the athlete
from the hot environment and, once appropriate, restore oral
rehydration. If oral rehydration is not possible due to nausea,
parenteral (non-or al method such as intravenous) rehydration may
be consider ed (58).

cooling in an ice bath may be indicated (12). It is important to
immediately respond to symptoms of heat exhaustion to avoid
progression to heatstr oke.

environments and ensuring proper hydration before, during, and

Heatstroke
Heatstroke , a progression of heat exhaustion, is consider ed a
medical emer gency (12, 58). Heatstr oke occurs f rom intense or long-
term exercise and is characteriz ed by body temper ature
dysregulation greater than heat exhaustion. Signs and symptoms of
heatstr oke include rapid heart rate, hypotension, sweating with
possible evaporation of sweat by time of incident, hyperv entilation,
altered mental state, diarrhea, seizur e, and coma (12, 58).
Immediate recogni tion of heatstr oke is vital to preventing lethal
consequences; core body temper atures in excess of 109.4  °F (43 °C)
have been associated with death. Immediate total body cooling
measur es must be implemented such as total body immersion in ice.
Monitoring of core body temper ature is paramount to appropriately
responding to heatstr oke. Contacting emergency medical services is
indicated in cases wher e medical staf fing is not pr esent.
Prevention strategies include proper acclimation to training in hot
environments and ensuring proper hydration before, during, and
after training. A key elemen t of prevention resides in recognizing
that heat illness exists on a continuum of events all related to
dysregulation of the body’s thermor egulatory capaci ty. As such,
heatstr oke is likely to be preceded by heat cramps, syncope, and
heat exhaustion. Recognizing the presentation of heat cramps or
heat exhaustion bef ore heatstr oke is par amount to pr evention.

Cold Exposure
Cold exposur e, or hypothermia , occurs when the body’s
temper ature drops below 95 °F (35 °C), causing the physiological
systems to reduce functions and eventual ly shut down (18, 50).
While mild hypothermia is manageable, more severe forms are a
medical emergency . Hypothermia affects all organ systems, and
early symptoms include shivering; pale, dry skin; and increased
muscle tone (18). Increased heart rate, blood pressure, and
respiration may also be present as the body attempts to promote
thermogenesis (18). Risk factors for hypothermia include exercising
in cold temper atures without adequate warming methods as well as
exposur e to rain or cold water that results in wet clothing, which
may promote f urther heat loss.
Upon recognizing symptoms of cold exposur e, immediate
measur es for warming the body are required through environmental
temper ature and use of clothing. Monitoring body temper ature is a
necessi ty once hypothermia is recogniz ed; core tempe rature less
than 89.6 °F (32 °C) ma y lead to car diovascular f ailure (18).
It is importa nt to recognize that although the body can
acclimate to training in hot conditions, there is little to
no acclimation to training in cold environments.
Prevention strategies include recognizing extremes in
temperature exposure and use of protective clothing to
help create a warming effect while training.

Conclusion
Effective athletic injury rehabi litation and recondi tioning require
efficient communication between the members of the allied health
team. Each member plays a distinct role in ensuring the injured
athlete’ s return to function; although the job of each professional is
different, the tasks of each team member complement those of the
others on the team. Goals are establ ished for each athlete, and
therapeutic exercise programs must be designed, adminis tered, and
progressed according to the tissue healing sequence. The program
must be individual ized to effectively return the athlete to normal
function and competi tive athletics. Early rehabi litation for initial
tissue protection and strengthening is more structur ed than later
rehabi litation phases; the latter allows for progression to functional
activities that are specific to the athlete’ s sport and position.
Designing reconditioning programs for injured athletes necessi tates
a careful examination of the sport requirements and a thorough
understanding of both the heal ing pr ocess and ther apeutic ex ercise.

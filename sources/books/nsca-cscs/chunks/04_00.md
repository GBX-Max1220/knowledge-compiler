Historical Perspective on Interest in the
Endocrine System
The significance of the endocrine system in strength and
condi tioning gained prominence with the rise of anabol ic drug use in
the 1940s and 1950s (7, 58). Fahey and colleagues were the first to
investigate how resistance exercises affected endogenous hormone
concentr ations, specifically testoster one, across different sexes,
ages, and training statuses,  paving the way for ongoing resear ch
into hormonal responses to resistance exercise and training (23). In
1988, a series of reviews were published from the first symposium
on the physiolo gy of resistance exercise and training at the American
College of Sports Medicine ’s annual meeting. As part of that
symposium set of papers, Kraemer (97) overviewed the sparse
number of studies that had been done on the various hormonal
responses to resistance exercise and training. This stimulated a line
of hormonal resear ch that continues today to explor e further the
mechanisms behind these acute responses and training adaptations
(46). Now the body of literature consists of thousands of studies on
this topic.

Endocrine Terminology, Functions, and
Mechanisms
Grasping the effects of resistance exercise on hormonal responses
requires a solid understanding of endocrine system terminology .
While the learning curve is significant, becoming familiar with this
language is vital for effectively translating scienti fic insights into
practical training strategies. Hormones , the chemical  messengers
or signal molecules, are synthesized, often stored, and released into
the blood by endocrine glands  and certain other cells (figure 4.1,
table 4.1). Neurons also synthesiz e, store, and secrete
neurotransmi tters, which may perform hormonal function s, a study
known as neuroendocrinology —exploring interactions between
the nervous and endocrine systems. Typical ly, endocrine glands are
prompted to release hormones by a chemical signal received by
receptors on the gland or by direct neural stimulation. For example,
the adrenal medul la, the inner part of the adrenal gland, releases
the hormone epinephrine in response to sympathe tic neural
stimulation from the brain (68, 74, 87, 129). Mean while, the outer
layer of the adrenal cortex produces and releases cortisol when
stimulated by adrenocorticotr opic hormone (ACTH) from the pituitary
gland (75, 78, 90). Once released, these hormones travel in the
bloodstr eam to specific receptors located on the surface (for peptide
hormones) or inside the target tissue  cells (for steroid  and
thyroid horm ones ) (4, 5, 6, 8, 22, 39), facilitating a targeted and
efficient r esponse.

Understanding Signaling Concepts
Different types of workouts trigger specific, comple x cellular
endocrine signal ing processes, thus highlighting the importance of
the selection for each of the five acute program variables and then
the total amount of work performed in a workout (70) for the
endocrine response. Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) primari ly directs
protein synthesis, but the regulation and expression of DNA affect
many other cellular functions  and the synthesis of various  molecules
essential for life. Hormonal signal ing processes begin when glands
release hormon es during, or in anticipation of, a resistance exercise
workout. These hormones then bind to specific receptors in muscle
cells and other cells. This binding initiates a cascade of responses,
gener ally resulting in altered cellular metabol ism or DNA
transcription modulation, which can affect protein synthesis. It is
essential to know that every cell and many subcel lular organel les
have genomic and non-geno mic receptors, and this is where the
hormone signals its message to make proteins. Here is a simplified
breakdown of the signal ing pr ocess f or skeletal muscle:
1. Stimulating the muscle . Resistance exercise activates muscles,
prompting a hormonal r esponse.
2. Hormonal response . Hormones are released and travel to
muscle cel ls.
3. Hormone–r eceptor interaction. Horm ones bind to specific
receptors in the cell membr ane of muscle cells (peptide
hormones) or inside the muscle cells (steroid hormones),
signal ing the need f or action inside the cel l.
4. Intracellular signal ing. This binding triggers a series of events
that lead to DNA signal ing inside the nucleus.
5. DNA translation . DNA instructions are transcribed to
messenger ribonucleic acid (mRNA), which carries the
instructions to the cel l’s protein-making machinery (ribosome).

6. Protein production . The mRNA instructions are translated into
specific proteins, the building blocks for muscle repair and
growth.
7. Muscle growth and repair. Regular training uses this muscle
development and repair mechanism, which is essentia l for
developing lean muscle mass and athletic perf ormance.

Functions of the Endocrine System in
Resistance Exercise and Training
The major functions of the endocrine system can be classified into
the following k ey hormonal actions:
Anabol ic responses (to build up). Hormones such as
testoster one, insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), and growth
hormone pla y critical r oles in muscle gr owth and r epair.
Permissiv e actions (to allow). Hormones such as insulin and
thyroid hormo nes enable critical metabol ic and physiolo gical
activities by enhancing nutrient uptak e and promoting
essential chem ical reactions  in muscles, thereby supporting
muscle gr owth and o verall metabol ic functions.
Catabol ic processes (to break down).  For example, cortisol
helps manage energy during stress but can hinder muscle
growth if not properly managed through adequate rest and
recovery. This emphasiz es the balance needed in training
regimens.
Types of Hormonal Release Mechanisms
Hormones are typical ly released into the bloodstr eam via the classic
endocrine pathway after gland stimulation. Hormones  can also
operate through intracrine, autocrine, and paracrine mechanisms,
which do not rely on the bloodstr eam. Intracrine and autocrine
actions involve hormones either binding to internal receptors within
their producing cell or being released to bind to surface receptors on
the same cell. An example of this is insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-
1) and its splice variant mechano growth factor (MGF), which muscle
fibers produce in response to mechanical stress or interaction with
growth hormones, affecting the muscle cell directly. Paracrine action
occurs when hormones affect adjacent cells, again bypassing the
bloodstr eam.

Binding Proteins
Hormones are often transported in the blood by various binding
proteins (4, 6), which serve as a tempor ary storage location and
protect the hormones from degradation, thereby exten ding their
lifetime in the body (called the half-life ). It is important to note that
hormones typically exert their effects only when they are released,
and thus are “free,” from these proteins. Howev er, some binding
proteins, such as sex hormone–binding globul in (SHBG), which binds
testoster one and estrogen, can initiate cellular responses themselv es
by activating pathways, including the cyclic adenosine
monophosphate (cAMP) pathway (29). These interactions are crucial
for understanding endocrine regulation and continue as an area of
study (101).
Target Cells and Tissues
Hormones influence a wide array of cells by interacting with
receptors throughout the body (1, 2, 57, 59, 62, 63). While our focus
is on skeletal  muscle tissue, it is important to recogniz e that
resistance exercise–induced hormone release also affects bone,
other connectiv e tissues, the kidneys, the liver, immune cells, and
neural tissues,  including the brain. Interestingly , not only do these
target cells in the different tissues adapt to the exercise stress, but
their r eceptors also gain a posi tive adaptiv e effect wi th training.

Importance of Recruitment of Muscle
Tissue
Henneman’ s size principle shows that as the resistance increases
(i.e., heavier loads), more and larger muscle fibers are recruited to
meet the increased force demand. This principle outlines the
recruitment order of motor units—from smaller, slow-twitch fiber
motor units to larger, fast-twitch fiber motor units. The heavier the
resistance, the greater the number and size of motor units recruited
in a muscle to produce force (77). While anabol ic and catabol ic
hormones maintain homeostatic balance by interacting with all cells,
stress causes hormonal chang es that reflect the body’s need to meet
new metabol ic demands and recovery needs. Howev er, only
recruited motor units adapt to the training stimulus and are affected
by the endocri ne system’ s increase in anabol ic hormones , likely due
to the increased activation of more receptors. Interestingly, and of
importance, increased catab olic hormone responses to stress or
distress can affect all tissues even if not recruited for exercise
performance. A catab olic state is highly detrimental for an athlete,
especial ly when cortisol levels remain chronical ly elevated. Combined
with the presence of reactiv e oxygen species (free radicals)
gener ated by the body’s inflammatory response, this condi tion
significantly impairs r ecovery and hampers perf ormance (77).

Muscle as the Target for Hormone
Interactions
Resistance exercise and training leads to muscle remodel ing through
a comp lex network of metabol ic and cellular signals. This process
involves muscle fiber damage, an inflammatory response, and the
breakdown of damaged proteins, ultimately synthesizing new
proteins integr ated into muscle structur es. The inflammatory phase,
which involves the immune  system, including T and B cells, is
significantly influenced by hormonal activity, emphasizing the role of
neuroendocrine immunolo gy in understanding how the nervous,
endocrine, and immune systems inter act in muscle adaptation (77).
Hormones regulate the balance between protein synthesis and
degradation, playing a critical role in muscle adaptation after
resistance exercise. They stimulate the production of contractile
proteins like actin and myosin, as well as structur al proteins such as
titin, which are essential for muscle function. Anabolic hormones ,
including insulin, insulin-like growth factors (IGFs), testoster one, and
GH, are particularly vital because they drive tissue growth and
counter act the effects of catabolic hormones  like cortisol and
progester one, which can lead to protein degradation. Furthermor e,
hormones like thyroid hormones play a supportiv e, permissiv e role,
facilitating the actions of other hormones. The potential ly negativ e
effects of cortisol in recovery, such as inhibi ting immune cell activity
and disrupting pathways like the Akt/mechanistic target of
rapam ycin (mTOR) pathway—important for initiating mRNA
translation— are also notable, demonstr ating the complex
interactions between hormones and muscle fibers that influence
muscle f unction and adaptation to tr aining.

Role of Receptors in Me diating
Hormonal Changes
The effectiveness of hormonal signals depends entirely on the
presence of hormone-speci fic receptors on or in the target cells. This
ensur es that each hormone only affects its intended cells and tissues
(those that express the specific receptors). Such specificity is crucial
for maintaining precise hormonal regulation within the body. Since
many hormones cannot directly cross the cell membr ane, they rely
on binding to membr ane receptors that cause secondary
messengers  to transmi t signals internal ly, including reaching the
DNA in the cell’s nuclei. These receptors, located either in the cell
membr ane for polypeptide hormones  or within the cytosol for
steroid and thyroid hormones, allow various cells, from muscle fibers
to immune and br ain cel ls, to r espond to hormonal signals.
A fundamental principle in endocrinology , often described through
the lock-and-key model  (24), explains  how a hormo ne (the key)
must precisely match a receptor (the lock) to trigger a response
(figure 4.2). Howev er, this model oversimpl ifies the complexi ty of
hormone–r eceptor interactions. For example, cross-reactivity
allows recepto rs to engage with hormones other than their primary
matches, and allosteric binding  can modify how a receptor
responds to its primary hormone (such as blocking binding to the
receptor and thus signal ing). Additional ly, receptors might have
allosteric sites where nonhormonal substances can enhance or
reduce the hormonal respons e. Some hormones, like GH, might even
need to aggregate into specific forms (multiple hormones bound
together in a “cluster ”) to effectively signal through their receptors,
as shown in studies using the tibial line assay (40), indicating that
specific molecular configur ations ar e essential f or their activi ty.

Adaptation Limits and Receptor Sensitivity
When a muscle fiber has reached its capaci ty for protein
incorpor ation, or when a chronical ly elevated concentr ation of a
hormone causes overstimulation, receptors may become
desensi tized or unresponsiv e—a condi tion known as receptor
downregulation ; a classic example of this phenomenon is insulin
resistance, which can be caused by chronical ly elevated circulating
insulin concentr ations. The reduced signal from the hormone
following overstimulation signifies a reduced ability of the hormone
to interact with its receptor  or downr egulation of the number of
receptors, which can effectively stop further cellular responses and
adaptations (37, 122). As such, receptors can adjust their affinity for
binding, and the number of available receptors can vary. These
changes affect cellular adaptation as significantly , if not more, as
changes in hormone concentr ations. For example, heavy resistance
overtraining can lead to a downr egulation of beta-2 receptors,
affecting their interaction with epinephrine and reducing muscle
contractile force (37). Exploring how receptor characteristics alter in
response to training and affect muscle adaptation is key to
understanding the complex interactions between hormones and
exercise.

Categories of Hormones
Hormones can be classi fied into thr ee main categories based on their
molecular structure: steroid, peptide (or polypeptide), and amine
hormones.
Steroid Hormone Interactions
Steroid hormo nes, including  cortisol, testoster one, and estradiol
produced by the adrenal cortex and/or gonads, can diffuse across
cell membr anes due to their lipophi lic (fat-soluble) nature. They
engage with specific recepto rs inside the cell to form a hormone–
receptor complex (H-R C). This complex under goes a
conformational change, activates, and pairs with another H-RC. It
then moves to the nucleus, interacts with the DNA, and promotes
the transcription process for specific proteins by “unzipping” DNA
and identi fying regulatory elements of the targeted gene (18, 117).
The resulting mRNA is then translated by ribosom es in the
sarcoplasm into the intended proteins. The steroid hormon e–induced
protein synthesis pr ocess is depicted in figure 4.3.
Testoster one’s influence on muscle cells encompasses more than
its genomic effects. In addition to the primary nuclear receptor , a
membr ane receptor for testoster one has also been described in
detail (128). Such a membr ane receptor allows testoster one to affect
the cell through rapid and nongenomic interactions (i.e., those that
do not involve DNA transcription in the nuclei) within various other
cellular compartments. These interactions play a crucial role in
muscle adaptation, recovery, and growth by facilitating the
production of essential contractile and structur al proteins,
modulating calcium flux in muscle cells, altering membr ane fluidity,
and activating key signal ing pathways (e.g., the MAPK/ERK
pathway). Such nongenomic actions underscor e testoster one’s
multifaceted role in muscle development. Understanding
testoster one’s genomic and nongenomic effects is vital for a

compr ehensiv e grasp of steroid hormones’ impact on muscle biology

Polypeptide Hormone Interactions
Polypeptide hormones are composed of chains of amino acids and
include hormon es such as GH and insulin. Due to their non-lipophi lic
nature, polypeptide hormones cannot cross the cell membr ane, and,
therefore, their effects are mediated by secondary messengers
within the cell. When these hormones bind to their membr ane
receptor , they cause a conformational change that allows the
receptor to transmi t the hormonal signal inside the cell. This
transmission initiates a cascade of signal ing events that, among
others, can affect metabol ic activities, influence DNA transcription, or
kick-start mRNA translation at the ribosome. For example, insulin
activates a pathway that results in glucose transporters (speci fically
GLUT4) migrating from the cytosol to the cell membr ane, enhancing
the cell’s uptak e of glucose (45). This is crucial for managing blood
glucose lev els and cel lular substr ate availability for ATP pr oduction.

Training-Mediated Hormonal Responses
and Mechanisms
Electrical, chemical, and hormonal signals are transmi tted from the
brain, active muscles, and other tissues to various endocrine glands,
preparing the body’s physiological systems for the demands of the
workout (20, 23, 28, 32, 35, 49, 75, 80, 81). Heavy resistance
training over months to years significantly enhances musculatur e
size, strength, and power , mediated partly through adaptiv e
hormonal responses (47, 50, 70, 73, 76, 88, 95). These adaptations
arise from increased levels of anabol ic hormones such as
testoster one, growth hormone, and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-
1) that acutely rise during exercise and remain elevated after
exercise. Additional ly, resistance exercise leads to an upregulation of
hormone receptors in muscle tissues, which increases the muscle’ s
sensitivity to these hormones and enhances protein synthesis and
muscle hypertr ophy. The structur al changes in muscle and neural
adaptations contribute to increased strength and power , including
improvements in the rate of force production and alterations in
muscle fiber recruitment patterns. Resistance training also positively
affects bone density and the strength of connectiv e tissues,
supporting increased muscular strength and overall physical
performance. One critical pathway in muscle protein synthesis
activated by resistance training is the mammal ian target of the
rapam ycin (mTOR) pathway, which is crucial for muscle growth.
These complex interactions result in adaptiv e changes in skeletal
muscle and other tissues, enhancing the activation of muscle
functions o ver time.
Acute hormonal releases reflect the nature and intensi ty of
physiological stress, such as insulin-related metabol ic demands,
prompting adjustments in resting metabol ism. These coordinated
hormonal responses are critical for adapting to training programs,
influencing tissue remodel ing, and promoting anabol ic actions.

Interpreting Hormonal Changes in
Peripheral Blood
Understanding exercise effects on the body goes beyond physical
observ ations; it includes reading studies that monitor biochemical
changes such as hormone fluctuations in blood, muscle, and
surrounding fluids (114). These changes provide insights into the
body’s stress response and adaptation to exercise. Howev er,
interpr eting these changes can be complex, because hormone levels
in the blood are just one aspect of a broader hormona l response
that also includes neural and nutritional factors such as being fasted
or not and if proteins and carboh ydrates are ingested around a
workout. Increases in blood hormone levels gener ally indicate
enhanced release from endocrine glands, potential ly boosting
hormone–r eceptor interactions in target tissues. Yet the actual
impact depend s on the state and quanti ty of the receptors —whether
they are primed to bind hormones and initiate crucial processes
involved in protein synthesis, including activating pathways such as
mTOR (11, 13, 14, 66). Conversely , decreases in hormone levels
might suggest increased tissue uptak e, faster hormone breakdown,
or reduced secr etion.
Several physiological factors , including circadian rhythms, fluid
volume changes, tissue clearance rates, venous pooling, and
interactions with binding proteins, also influence observ ed circulating
hormone conce ntrations after exercise. These factors determine the
hormone’ s efficacy in interacting with receptors and triggering
cellular responses, underl ining the complexi ty of hormone function
and its role in ex ercise adaptations.
While increased hormone levels after exercise indicate heightened
glandular activity, they do not directly correlate with muscle gains.
Muscle size and strength can improve without significant changes in
hormone levels by, for example, using heavy 1RM to 3RM zones with
long rest periods of over 3 minutes. Such resistance loads recruit
250
maximal amounts of motor units and enhance receptor binding.
Howev er, such workouts’ relatively low metabol ic demands typical ly
result in only modest, if any, increases in blood concentr ations of
testoster one, IGF-1, and growth hormone. Nevertheless, slight
hormonal changes, combined with paracrine and autocrine  signal ing,
are generally sufficient to effectively signal via receptors  and other
molecular pathways within tissues in the neuromuscular system to
stimulate growth and repair. Conversely , a workout that involves
8RM to 10RM resistances, short rest periods of 1 to 2 minutes, and a
higher volume of work produces greater metabol ic demands on the
body. The metabol ic stress is evidenced by significant increases in
blood lactate and a surge in both anabol ic and catabol ic hormones.
These hormonal changes signal skeletal muscle receptors effectively,
even with the recruitment of fewer motor units using lighter
resistances. Periodization strategies that incorpor ate a range of
different resistances over time ensur e effective muscle activation
across various workout styles, where hormones help mediate
changes. Such workout design variations reflect the complex nature
of exercise-ind uced hormon al activation and its contribution to
adaptiv e changes in the body .
Hormone responses in the blood are tightly linked to the
characteristics of the resistance exercise protocol.

Primary Anabolic Hormones in Musc le
Development
Key anabol ic hormones that drive muscle tissue growth and
remodel ing include testoster one, growth hormone, and insulin-like
growth factors (IGFs), with additional roles played by insulin and
thyroid hormones (25, 26, 27 , 28, 38, 91).
Testosterone’s Role in Musc le Adaptation
Testoster one, the primary androgen affecting skeletal muscle, and
dihydrotestoster one, which acts mainly on sex-linked tissues, play
crucial roles in the body. While circulating testoster one levels
indicate anabol ic activity, the actual binding of testoste rone to its
receptor is vital for stimulatin g muscle growth. Binding to androgen
genomic and non-genomic receptors activates various signal ing
pathways that enhance gene expression for muscle hypertr ophy and
suppr ess those  involved in protein degradation. Therefore, increased
testoster one levels due to increased motor unit activation indicate
metabol ic demands beyond homeostatic condi tions and are
associated wi th enhanced r eceptor binding (124 , 133).
Testoster one also indirectly affects muscle growth by promoting
the release of growth hormone from the pituitary, which influences
muscle protein synthesis. This hormone’ s relationship with growth
hormone is either permissiv e or syner gistic in promoting protein
synthesis (103). Furthermor e, testoster one affects the nervous

The adrenal gland is integr al to the body’s adaptation to stress,
particularly during and after exercise. Structur ally, it consists of two
main compone nts: the medul la and the cortex, each responding to
stress in distinct ways. The adrenal medul la, the inner core of the

acute stress. It rapidly secretes catecholamines such as epinephrine

immediate stress responses and immune enhancement (129). The
adrenal cortex is stimulated by adrenocorticotr opic hormone (ACTH)
from the anterior pituitary gland to produce and release cortisol.

metabol ic responses to stress. As such, cortisol is regulated on a
systemic level by the hypoth alamic -pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and
locally within tissues by enzymes such as 11β-hydroxy- steroid
dehydrogenase (11β-HSD) to maintain glucocorticoid balance (25,
27, 99). Cortisol is crucial for sustaining energy during prolonged
stress by facilitating glucose availability through selectiv e glucose
sparing and mobilization, gluconeogenesis, and fat and protein
catabol ism. Howev er, prolonged elevation of cortisol levels—if not
returned to baseline within 24 hours—can adversely affect the body,
because cortis ol in this context is consider ed catabol ic and anti-
anabol ic. Sustained high cortisol can impair immune function, affect
fat metabol ism and deposi tion, reduce protein synthesis, increase
protein degradation, and interfere with anabol ic signal ing pathways,
thereby promoting a catabol ic state that can undermine muscle
repair and growth (75, 87, 89, 92, 129). Interference in anabol ic
signal ing is partly due to the cortisol significantly contributing to
immune suppr ession observ ed after intense exercise stress. Cortisol
is crucial in “inactiv ating” immune cell functions during recovery

phases, vital for post-exercise muscle remodel ing and overall

Cortisol is critical in energy balance, immune regulation, and

prolonged exposur e. Athletes show a refined capabi lity to convert

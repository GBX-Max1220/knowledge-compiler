Steroid Hormone Interactions
Steroid hormo nes, including  cortisol, testoster one, and estradiol
produced by the adrenal cortex and/or gonads, can diffuse across
cell membr anes due to their lipophi lic (fat-soluble) nature. They
engage with specific recepto rs inside the cell to form a hormone–
receptor complex (H-R C). This complex under goes a
conformational change, activates, and pairs with another H-RC. It
then moves to the nucleus, interacts with the DNA, and promotes
the transcription process for specific proteins by “unzipping” DNA
and identi fying regulatory elements of the targeted gene (18, 117).
The resulting mRNA is then translated by ribosom es in the
sarcoplasm into the intended proteins. The steroid hormon e–induced

Polypeptide Hormone Interactions
Polypeptide hormones are composed of chains of amino acids and
include hormon es such as GH and insulin. Due to their non-lipophi lic
nature, polypeptide hormones cannot cross the cell membr ane, and,
therefore, their effects are mediated by secondary messengers
within the cell. When these hormones bind to their membr ane
receptor , they cause a conformational change that allows the
receptor to transmi t the hormonal signal inside the cell. This
transmission initiates a cascade of signal ing events that, among
others, can affect metabol ic activities, influence DNA transcription, or
kick-start mRNA translation at the ribosome. For example, insulin
activates a pathway that results in glucose transporters (speci fically
GLUT4) migrating from the cytosol to the cell membr ane, enhancing
the cell’s uptak e of glucose (45). This is crucial for managing blood
glucose lev els and cel lular substr ate availability for ATP pr oduction.

Amine Hormone Interactions
Amine hormones are indeed synthesiz ed from amino acids, including
those derived from tyrosine (such as epinephrine, norepinephrine,
and dopamine) and tryptophan (such as serotonin), and primari ly
exert their effects by binding to membr ane receptors. These
hormones typical ly act through secondary messenger systems,
similar to polypeptide  hormones. For example, epinephrine and
norepinephrine interact with G protein-coupled receptors to initiate
signal ing cascades in volving cAMP or other secondary messengers.

Training-Mediated Hormonal Responses
and Mechanisms
Electrical, chemical, and hormonal signals are transmi tted from the
brain, active muscles, and other tissues to various endocrine glands,
preparing the body’s physiological systems for the demands of the
workout (20, 23, 28, 32, 35, 49, 75, 80, 81). Heavy resistance
training over months to years significantly enhances musculatur e
size, strength, and power , mediated partly through adaptiv e
hormonal responses (47, 50, 70, 73, 76, 88, 95). These adaptations
arise from increased levels of anabol ic hormones such as
testoster one, growth hormone, and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-
1) that acutely rise during exercise and remain elevated after
exercise. Additional ly, resistance exercise leads to an upregulation of
hormone receptors in muscle tissues, which increases the muscle’ s
sensitivity to these hormones and enhances protein synthesis and
muscle hypertr ophy. The structur al changes in muscle and neural
adaptations contribute to increased strength and power , including
improvements in the rate of force production and alterations in
muscle fiber recruitment patterns. Resistance training also positively
affects bone density and the strength of connectiv e tissues,
supporting increased muscular strength and overall physical
performance. One critical pathway in muscle protein synthesis
activated by resistance training is the mammal ian target of the
rapam ycin (mTOR) pathway, which is crucial for muscle growth.
These complex interactions result in adaptiv e changes in skeletal
muscle and other tissues, enhancing the activation of muscle
functions o ver time.
Acute hormonal releases reflect the nature and intensi ty of
physiological stress, such as insulin-related metabol ic demands,
prompting adjustments in resting metabol ism. These coordinated
hormonal responses are critical for adapting to training programs,
influencing tissue remodel ing, and promoting anabol ic actions.

Interpreting Hormonal Changes in
Peripheral Blood
Understanding exercise effects on the body goes beyond physical
observ ations; it includes reading studies that monitor biochemical
changes such as hormone fluctuations in blood, muscle, and
surrounding fluids (114). These changes provide insights into the
body’s stress response and adaptation to exercise. Howev er,
interpr eting these changes can be complex, because hormone levels
in the blood are just one aspect of a broader hormona l response
that also includes neural and nutritional factors such as being fasted
or not and if proteins and carboh ydrates are ingested around a
workout. Increases in blood hormone levels gener ally indicate
enhanced release from endocrine glands, potential ly boosting
hormone–r eceptor interactions in target tissues. Yet the actual
impact depend s on the state and quanti ty of the receptors —whether
they are primed to bind hormones and initiate crucial processes
involved in protein synthesis, including activating pathways such as
mTOR (11, 13, 14, 66). Conversely , decreases in hormone levels
might suggest increased tissue uptak e, faster hormone breakdown,
or reduced secr etion.
Several physiological factors , including circadian rhythms, fluid
volume changes, tissue clearance rates, venous pooling, and
interactions with binding proteins, also influence observ ed circulating
hormone conce ntrations after exercise. These factors determine the
hormone’ s efficacy in interacting with receptors and triggering
cellular responses, underl ining the complexi ty of hormone function
and its role in ex ercise adaptations.

Short-Term Goals
In addition to the process and outcome distinction just discussed,
goals can also be categoriz ed as short-term and long-term. Short-
term goals are gener ally those that are directly related to current
training or competi tion and are guidel ines that can be attained in a
relatively short time frame. Short-term goals also increase the
likelihood of success because, although challenging, they are
relatively close to the athlete’ s present ability level. They also
increase confidence, self-efficacy , and motiv ation because of the
likelihood of success. In this regard, short-term process and outcome
goals counteract the boredom and frustration that are potential side
effects of long, ar duous tr aining r egimens.

Finally, the specificity of the goals— whether short- or long-term—

Optimal goal setting requires knowledge of the exercise sciences
in both the biophysical and behavioral domains. The efficacy of goals
for improving athletic performance lies in their relevance to the
physical needs of the athlete. For example, formulating a series of
appropriate goals to enable a 400-meter runner to decrease his or
her time in the event rests on understanding the physical profile,
relevant metabol ic pathways, and biomechanical technique to be
developed.

Yips, Choking, Flow, and Clutch
Often coaches struggle with helping athletes with being less anxious when they
compete. Anxiet y can interfere with fluid movement patterns. This is known as the
yips, the twisties , or, during critical moments,  choking . The more technical term is
focal dystonia .
Simone Biles descri bes her twisties as a moment when she started thinking and
lost air awareness and propriocepti on as she rotated during a gymnastics
competi tion (58a). “If I still had my air awareness, and I just was having a bad
day, I would have continued.… But it was more than that.… It’s so danger ous.…
It’s a miracle I landed on my feet.… As soon as I landed that vault, I went and told
my coach that I cannot continue. ” She expressed a deficit mental focus that
created spatial disorientation. Rick Ankiel describes this during his Major League
Basebal l pitching career like this: “I was pitching with fear … That put me into a
freeze mindset, and that’s what led to the yips.” Closely related but distinct,
choking is when focal dystonia happens during a critical time during performance
execution.
Resear chers have suggested that perfectionism, social comparison, and dystonia
are associ ated with choking. Opposed to this experience is the positive emotional
state of flow. Athletes describe flow as effortless movement, complete trust in

Mental Health and Strength and
Conditioning
Mental health  is emerging as an important topic for strength and
condi tioning professionals. Resear ch published related to mental
health and athletes has accelerated from roughly 74 publications in
2003 to over 1,100 in 2023. This growth demonstr ates increased
interest in the topic, but resear ch outcomes support the need to
focus on building competence in strength and condi tioning because
mental health affects performance (24, 62). In a study, student -
athletes were at risk for depression (22.3%), anxiet y (12.5%), and
low self-esteem (8%) (62). While most resear ch has reported higher
rates of positive mental health for athletes compar ed to nonathletes,
training for and playing sports are stressful (31, 47). Some of the
stressors includ e burnout from long hours of training; social identi ty
pressure to perform well; and meeting high expectations from
coaches, teammates, and self while attempting to compete with
other athletes. These stressors can affect mental health and hygiene
that can appear at subcl inical lev els in student -athletes.
What is mental health? Defining mental health encompasses
emotional, psychological, and social well-being (5, 47, 62). Also,
mental health is not just the absence of a mental illness. Challenges
to mental health are often detected when sport, training, and life
challenges disrupt normal self-care behavior. Coaches working with
athletes will have varied backgr ounds related to psychological
distress identi fication and mental health management. Some
practical parameters for coaching education related to mental health
issues ar e needed.
Strength and condi tioning professionals can observ e signs and
symptoms of changing mental health in athletes. While striving to
enhance performance, a holistic athlete monitoring program can
address self-care, recovery, and optimization of psychological,
behavioral, and emotional f actors (5).

A clear understanding of similarities and differences between
mental states can be useful. It is important to note that several
nuances of anxiet y exist and can include gener al anxiet y disorder,
social anxiet y, panic disorders, and phobias. Anxiet y often presents
as a circular pattern of thoughts and feelings of heightened
importance known as ruminations  and worry . Some predisposing
factors for anxiet y include large social and schedule adjustments, a
biological family history of anxiet y, negativ e life events, early trauma,
and shyness in childhood (6, 62). Lists of signs and symptoms of
anxiet y, depression, and stress in athletes are in table 9.1. These
manifest in varied somatic, cogni tive, and behavioral symptoms.
Often maladaptiv e coping and perceptions of impending outcomes or
doom fill the individual with urgency , overwhelming feelings of
dread, and focus on negati ve consequences. By the end of this
section, strength and condi tioning professionals will be able to
identi fy signs and symptoms of anxiet y, depression, and stress;
recogniz e the appropriate means of support; and develop an
appreciation of the impact of mental health on performance and
injury risk.
Depression and anxiet y can share signs and symptoms. These
include agitation, somatic complaints (e.g., stomach aches or sweat y
palms), restles sness, social withdrawal, difficulty thinking,  and sleep
disturbance. These represent cogni tive processing biases that can
lead to rumination and worry (26). What sets depression apart is

anhedonia (lack of emotion), feelings of hopele ssness or
helplessness, and low energy (26). Studies have identi fied that
depression can begin with poor performance, injury , concussion,
social comparisons, high expectations, and changes in social or
playing status (26, 31). Also affecting depression are changes in
feedback from coaches, behavioral deactiv ation, negativ e self-
perceptions, evaluations, and feelings of helplessness or
hopelessness. Stress is an imbalance between the demand s of a task
or situation and the ability to cope with the challenge,  as stated
earlier in this chapter . Chronic stress often interferes with sleep,
mood, and self-care habits. Because mental stress is unique to each
individual, it has many signs and symptoms. The most recognizable
signs of str ess include ur gency , agitation, and hurried speech.
Now that these states have been identi fied, how do coaches help
athletes get the appropriate  support? One helpful way is a three-
step process that follows the content of Weinber g and Gould (64)
while adding the assessment of duration and disruption from more
traditional psychological evaluation. First, identi fy the signs and
symptoms of distress related to affect, behavior, and cogni tions.
Second, determ ine the duration of distress (lasting greater than 2-3
weeks) and potential disruption (e.g., sleep patterns, nutritional
habits, social relationships). Specifically, is the distress tempor ary or
persistent, and how will it affect behaviors outside of sports? Third,
determine how to support the athlete. A question could follow: Is it
important to refer the athlete for more support, allow the athlete
time for self-reflection, or collaborate with the athlete to work on
centering or environmental management to change disruptiv e
performance-r elated r esponses?
The first step in identi fying psychological signs of distress is
classifying the affect, behavior, and cogni tions (ABCs) observ ed in
athletes (11, 64, 65). Affect refers to the mood and emotions seen in
and reported by the athlete . For instance, an athlete might look
anxious, have a stressed face, report feeling hopeless, or have a
persistent negativ e expression. Behavior refers to movements like

nervous pacing, fidgeting, slow reactions, and rapid eye tracking.
Cogni tions are thoughts reported, negativ e worry about
performances or outcomes, overgener alizing, catastr ophizing, and
doubts about abilities or past failures. A brief review of cogni tions
that can disrupt performances are all-or-nothing thinking, jumping to
conclusions, magnification, minimization, emotional reasoning, and
confirmation bias (5, 22).
Once these signs are observ ed, step 2 is to determine if the issue
is temporary or persistent. To determine what level of help is
required for an athlete, coaches need to learn how the signs and
symptoms of distress affect performance and when and how to refer
to a pr ofessional who is tr ained to handle psy chological distr ess.
This third step, referral to a qualified support professional,
requires the coach to work in an individual setting with the athlete to
develop a plan to refer to a support professional, who should be
consul ted or contacted ahead of time. This remains a key skill to
assist athletes with self-care for mental health and as an important
step for coaches. A performance coach or consul tant is trained in
implementing psychological skills that are attention based and do
not enter into interpersona l or clinical issues. For example, a
performance coach or consul tant (usual ly certified through the
Association of Applied Sport Psychology as a certified mental
performance consul tant [CMPC]) emplo ys goal setting, imagery , self-
talk, attentional focus, or other psychological skills to direct effort
and attention. A counselor is licensed by the state and is usually
trained to help with interpersonal, cultural, and mental health issues.
For example, if an athlete needs a counselor , interpersonal and self-
care can become a focus of cogni tive behavioral treatment. Also
licensed by the state, a psychologist is trained to administer
psychological testing batteries, focusing on interpersonal and self-
care issues to work wi th chr onic mental heal th issues.

In the future, challenges to mental health in athletics could also
come from social media; name, image, and likeness (NIL); and
tempor ary commi tments or lack of identi fication with a program
facilitated by the college transfer portal. Thus, a higher level of social
comparison and self-esteem challenges will likely be stressors for
athletes. Imperative to the preparation for future strength and
condi tioning professionals will be skill building and resour ces related
to these emer ging chal lenges.
When subclinical anxiet y, depression, or stress are seen by the
coach, a plan can be made to change behavior, team environment,
and emotional management. Subcl inical issues include low-level and
short-term mental or emotional distress that are not serious enough
to affect athletes’ personal, academic, and social roles. These often
do not last longer than a week and do not disrupt sleep, eating, and
social functioning. Howev er, when persistent issues or disruptions
become more chronic and severe, a professional referral to a mental
health special ist is clearly warranted. When more chronic issues
arise, the coach should seek support of the sports medicine staff
(e.g., athletic trainers, nutritionists, team physicians) as well as the
mental health communi ty in a respectf ul way (maintaining
confidential ity, dignity, and communication with the athlete), so
prioritizing mental well-being is the first goal. By developing a
reference list of professionals in the school, surrounding communi ty,
or athletic department, the strength and condi tioning professional
can be bet ter pr epared for mental heal th issues should they arise.

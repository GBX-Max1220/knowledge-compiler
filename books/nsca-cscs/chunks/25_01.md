Organizing the construction and design of a strength and

Genera l Aspects of New Facility Design
Building a new facility takes significant time and planning. One of
the first things to do is assem ble a commi ttee of professionals. The
commi ttee should consist of a builder, architect, designers, lawyers,
financial departments, and people who will be involved in using the
facility. At least one strength and condi tioning professional, or other
expert who will be working in the facility, should be included in the
commi ttee to provide a persp ective that will help maximiz e the utility
and safety of the space. This commi ttee will assist with the facility
design as well as consider economic factors of openi ng a new
facility. One of the main challenges in designing a strength and
condi tioning facility is tailoring the facility to the intended audience.

Predesign Phase
The predesign phase is the first step in building a new facility. This
phase should consist of a needs analysis, a feasibi lity study, and
master plan formation. At the end of the predesign  phase, a
reputable architect should be hired—likely through a bid process—
and begin dev eloping a blueprint f or the f acility.
The needs analysis  is the step when the designers and experts
collaborate to determine the needs of the strength and condi tioning
program (10). Examples of questions are “How much open space do
1729
you need?” and “What equipment needs to fit into the allotted
space?” The needs analysis should be consistent with the specific
programming needs of the strength and condi tioning and athletic
departments. For example, if a strength and condi tioning
professional regularly incorpor ates jump testing on force plates
during weight room sessions,  an area needs to be set aside for that
activity. For those serving on the design commi ttee, the needs
analysis should convey an effective environment to properly
implement the intended program. While site, budget, construction,
and other limitations may require a certain level of flexib ility as the
project progresses, safety consider ations for facility users should not
be jeopar dized (2-7 , 9-17 , 19-22).
The second part of the predesign phase is called a feasibility
study . This process should analyz e strengths, weaknesses,
opportuni ties, and threats (known as a SWOT analysis ) (10). The
goal of the feasibility study is to ensur e that the financial investment
will yield a viable and sustainable return. The feasibi lity study should
also evaluate the location, strengths and weaknesses of any
proposed ideas, and the growth potential for strength and
condi tioning and related programs within the department. The
potential opportuni ties and competi tion threats also need to be
consider ed. This includes market evaluation to identi fy the best
opportuni ty to train athletes most efficiently , as well as analyzing the
target market and growth potential of competing facilities. In the
collegiate environment, this may include comparisons to other
facilities in the athletic conference or be related to athlete recruiting
goals. The aim of the feasibility study is to help determ ine if the
facility will have an opportuni ty for success against competing
programs and businesses.
The master plan is the gener al plan for all phases of the new
facility. The master plan should include the building and construction
plan, facility design, budget information, and an operational plan to
act on once the facility is completed (10). The operational plan
should consist of short-term and long-term goals that will enhance
1730
the success of the facility both in the near term and in the future.
The operational plan should  also include staff development and a
plan f or the hiring pr ocess.
Hiring an architect is the final step of the predesign phase.
Selecting an architect is crucial for meeting administr ation and
department goals. It is important to select from a list of architects
who have a positive reputation based on previous work. If possible,
it is best to choose an architect who has had previous experience in
the strength and condi tionin g industry . The architect will likely be
hired through a bid process, meaning that the architect who is hired
is often the one who subm its the lowest price. Bid plans require
efficient use of resour ces so that the money is spent on what is most
useful for the users of the f acility.
Design Phase
The design phase  is the second phase in the process of building a
new facility. In the design phase, ideas from the commi ttee come
together with regard to the structur e and design eleme nts of the
facility. It is extremely important to plan around the flow of the
facility while adhering to all relevant regulations and codes. Federal,
state, and city codes are often highly specific about aspects of the
design. City planning departments can provide the local guidel ines
and appl icable codes f or constructing a f acility.
The first step in the design phase is finalizing the design
commi ttee. To reiterate, the commi ttee should include strength and
condi tioning and other athletic department professionals with
experience in facility planning and design. This is also the phase
when the designers need to work closely with the architect to
produce a blueprint of the f acility.
The blueprint and design need to consider the equipment
specifications. This will help with the flow of the facility and will
influence how effectively people can move from one area of the
facility to another once it is operational. Traffic flow is one of the
most important aspects of designing the facility. Creating easy
1731
access for people will have the greatest effect on the function and
safety of the facility when multiple groups of athletes are present at
the same time. The flow of traffic should also allow coaches or
supervisors to have a clear view of the entire floor. The locations of
any structur al pillars, columns, or other obstructions must be
consider ed in the planning of equipment spaces to account for traffic
flow and ac cess to emer gency exi ts.
Construction Phase
The construction phase  is the period of time from the beginning
of building the facility until completion. This is usually the longest
phase of the process. During construction, the commi ttee needs to
continual ly refer to the master plan to ensur e that the project is on
track with regard to the established goals and design. Keeping the
project on track in relation to the deadl ine must also become a
priority. Delays in construction can add significant financial strain.
Having access to legal counsel is beneficial to making sure that
construction deadl ines are met and extra expenses and legal action
are avoided.
Preoperation Phase
The preoperation phase  consists of the final steps before the
facility can open. These steps include finishing the interior decor
(note that appear ance can greatly enhance the experience  of users),
aesthetic consider ations to support athlete recruiting or membership
enrollment, and hiring a staff of qualified professionals. The staff
should have at least the minimum required certifications and
education. For example, the National Collegiate Athletic Association
enforces regulations that require strength and conditioning
professionals to be certified by a national ly accredited organization.
In addition, many professional sport leagues and tactical positions
mandate that a strength and condi tioning professional is a Certified
Strength and Condi tioning Special ist (CSCS) through the National
Strength and Condi tioning Association (NSCA). Finding hardworking,
1732
knowledgeable, loyal, and trustworth y emplo yees is crucial to
running a successful strength and condi tioning program. During the
preoper ation phase, it is important to include a plan for staff
development as well. This could vary from semiannual workshops to
weekly staf f meetings.
During the preoper ation phase it is vital to create a plan that will
make opening the facility easy. For example, duties such as cleaning
and maintenance should be delegated to the staff on a weekly basis.
This is easier to do when a cleaning and maintenance schedule has
been cr eated and is f ollowed as soon as the f acility is oper ating.
Also during preoper ation, a plan for administr ative and clerical
duties should be created to organize work related to matters such as
liability insurance, schedul ing, and budgeting. Details, such as
choosing a schedul ing softw are package, installing an athlete
management system, and delegating clerical duties, should be
accounted f or bef ore the f acility opens.

Satellite Training Facilities
It has become common in recent years for college and professional
sport organizations to add alternativ e and sport-speci fic satellite
training facilities to accommodate the needs of growing strength and
condi tioning departments. Other examples of this include providing a
strength and condi tioning facility for visiting team use, as is
mandated by some professional sport leagues, or designing a studio
space for individual ized or small group training sessions. Special
attention should be given when planning satellite training facilities,
especial ly if existing, dated, or nontraining spaces are being
converted f or str ength and condi tioning use.
Satellite training facilities may benefi t from smaller pieces of
equipment based on the size of the space available (e.g., half rack
vs. full rack). In small facilities, it can be beneficial to install
multipurpose pieces of equipment rather than equipment with a
single function (9). A satellite training facility that does not have
dedicated walkways separ ating the equipment areas should be set
up with equipment bolted and secur ed around the perimeter . The
center of the room should remain open for transitions between
training stations while allowing unimpeded access to emergency
exits. As a rule, equipment and safety spacing should  never be
compr omised due to limited space. In accordance with the NSCA
Strength and Condi tioning Professional Standar ds and Guidel ines
(16), and as explained in this chapter , a minimally compliant
facility  must be in agreement with mandated building codes and
regulations, including accessibi lity (i.e., wheelchair access and ADA
compl iance), HVAC, electrical, lighting, flooring, storage, squar e
footage, safety spaces, and overhead clearance and must possess
well-maintained equipment and surf aces.

Outdoor Training Spaces
Weight room adjacent or satellite outdoor training spaces have
become more common in geogr aphical ly moder ate climates. In
addition, the global COVID-19 pandemic created increased
awareness among strength and condi tioning professionals for the
benefi t of social distancing, including the use of open- air outdoor
training spaces for enhanced ventilation (15). There are also
scenarios when schedul ing or programming can be improved by
having strength and condi tioning equipment available near sport or
practice facilities that do not have a dedicated weight room space.
While environmental factors, like weather , must always be
consider ed for athlete safety, this section provides recommendations
for the safe use of strength and condi tioning equipment in outdoor
training ar eas.
Outdoor training spaces may include multipurpose training lockers
or pods and be located next to a game or practice field (e.g., grass
or artificial turf) or in proximity to the weight room (e.g., via garage
door access). Outdoor training spaces may be for seasonal or year-
round use depending on climate. Important consider ations include
ensuring adequ ate space for strength and condi tioning activity to be
performed safely and that standar ds for equipment, safety spacing,
and walkways are clear. Outdoor training surfaces must offer proper
traction for athletic activity and should not be wet or damp from
recent storms. Ground surface temper atures should  also be
consider ed to avoid injury due to excessiv e heat or burns (e.g.,
artificial turf may have higher temper atures than surrounding
surfaces). Additional conside rations should be placed on ensuring
appropriate athletic footwear and attire for strength and condi tioning
sessions held outdoors.
Similar to indoor training spaces, outdoor training spaces should
have a posted emergency action plan that is understood by staff and
athletes. If training equipmen t is to be transported outsid e daily for
use, it is advisable for the coaching staff to make this task more

ergonomic by using a dolly, cart, or wagon with sturdy wheels. All
strength and condi tioning equipment should be inspected  regularly ,
especial ly if used outdoors, because being exposed to the elements
can ac celerate wear .

Assessing Athletic Program Needs
One of the more important consider ations in building a strength and
condi tioning facility is the needs and requirements of the athletes
and athletic program. The number of athletes, coaching
philosophies, athlete ages and experience levels, schedules and
availability, and equipment factors can affect the design of the
facility (12). When it is time to evaluate such needs, the facility
designers should be able to answer these questions:
How many athletes will use the facility? This is an important
consider ation because the facility size will be greatly
influenced by the number of athletes using the facility at the
same time (i.e., at peak usage) and the total number who use
it per day, week, and season. The number of athletes will also
affect the schedul ing and flow of the facility if two or more
groups are training simultaneously. Based on regulations such
as fire codes or other occupational health and safety
regulations, the city planning departments will specify the
maximum room capaci ty. In addition to the number of athletes
training, it is also essential to consider staffing and
supervision. The number of athletes being trained
simultaneously will affect the number of strength and
condi tioning professionals required to safely and effectively
monitor the tr aining session (16).
What are the training goals for the athletes, coaches, and
administr ation?  Training goals will directly affect equipment
selection for the facility. For example, if a strength and
condi tioning professional wants athletes to focus on
plyometrics and agility, the facility will need an area (turf or
field) designated for this purpose. The strength and
condi tioning professional should also use collective training
goals of the various stakeholders as a guidel ine for ordering
equipment. If resistance training is to be programmed, racks
1738
and platforms are more space efficient compar ed to bench or
incline bench pr ess setups that serv e only one purpose.
What are the demogr aphics of the athletes?  The demogr aphics
of the users are important when determining the equipment
needs of the facility. Are the users primari ly older, younger,
male, female, middle school, high school, college, or
professional? A facility with primari ly older populations may
not need as much free weigh t equipment as it does machines.
In a collegiate or team environment, use of the equipment
may be more efficient if it is grouped into sections so that
multiple groups can work out simultaneously without
interference.
What will the training experience of the athletes be like?
Answering this question will help the strength and condi tioning
professional devise training plans, which in turn will dictate the
equipment needed. Training will vary according to the
experience of the athlete. Athletes with no previous resistance
training experi ence may do more bodyweight exercises than
advanced athletes, who may do more weightl ifting. It is also
important to assess whether needs will change over time and
whether equipment requirements will vary depending on the
season.
How will training sessions be scheduled?  The schedul ing of
athletes or groups is one of the more difficult aspects of
running a facility. The numb er of people on staff, the layout,
and the design  of the facility can all be affected by the training
schedule. If more than one group will be using the facility at
one time, it would make sense to divide the facility into
sections so the amount of overlap is limited. Ideally, training
sessions should be scheduled throughout the day to avoid
capaci ty issues during the busiest or most popular times. This
will help maint ain suggested staff-to-athlete ratios and ensure
that enough equipment is available. (See chapter 26 for
additional detai ls.)

What technology is needed to administer training as intended?
Planning for technology is important to account for the
continued growth of the program. A strong Wi-Fi or Bluetooth
connection may be required if the intent is to deliver strength
and condi tioning programs using technology devices, such as
tablets, TV monitors, or cell phone apps. Ensuring access to a
strong cellular signal can provide an additional layer of safety
in case of an emergency . Additional ly, exercise testing and
monitoring equipment may require dedicated space to be
operated during training sessions and stored secur ely between
uses. This includes specific exercise testing devices, athlete
monitoring sensors, and accessories that may require special
cleaning, char ging, and maintenance.
What equipment needs to be repaired or modified? No
equipment in a facility should  have issues such as holes, cuts,
or missing pieces. If a cable is frayed or damaged, discontinue
using the cable column until the cable is replaced. All existing
equipment should be repaired or replaced if broken. Existing
equipment should be repaired promptly or should be sold to
provide funds for new equip ment. In some cases, a piece of
equipment is in good repair but needs an accessory for an
upgrade. For example, cable columns may require new
attachments to be f ully functional.
Once all of these questions are answer ed, the process of
designing the facility will be much easier . Ideally, the facility
designers should have a solution for every problem; this allows for
an optimal training environment. In reality, howev er, budget and
space limitations often stand  in the way. It is important to focus on
what will affect the facility the most and to design from there. For
example, not having enough space will have a larger impact on the
training environment and schedul ing than not having enough of a
special ized piece of equipmen t. The recommended minimu m amount
of space is 100 squar e feet (9.3 m2) per individua l, including all

staff (e.g. , athletic tr ainers) or persons pr esent (16).

Designing the Strength and
Conditioning Facility
Before arranging and positioning equipment, one must consider the
design of the strength and condi tioning facility, wheth er new or
existing. The strength and condi tioning professional should pay
particular attention to facility location and access, structur al and
functional considerations, environmental factors, and safety and
supervision.
Location
The strength and condi tionin g facility ideally should be located on
the ground floor away from offices and classr ooms. This will prevent
interruptions elsewher e in the building that are caused by dropped
weights, music, or other noises common to strength and
condi tioning facilities. If the facility is not located on the ground
floor, the floor must be stable enough to support heavy equipment
and equipment  that is dropped. The load-bearing capaci ty should be
at least 100 f oot-pounds per squar e foot (4 .8 kN/m2) (7).
Supervision Location
An office area for supervision should be in a central location with a
clear line of sight and windo ws or mirrors providing the opportuni ty
to look out and see everyone, including individuals and groups
entering or exiting the room. One option is to place the office at an
elevation o ver the floor of the weight r oom f or bet ter viewing.
Access
Every facility should be accessible to people with disabi lities, with
either a ramp or a wheelchair lift for any change in height exceeding
0.5 inches (1.3 cm). A ramp should run 12 inches for every 1-inch
rise (30.5 cm run for every 2.5 cm rise) with a landing at the top
and bottom (3), and stairs should have a rough strip on the edge to
1742
help prevent anyone from falling. Another option for persons with
disabi lities is a mechanical l ift or an elev ator.
Every facility should have at least one phone that is accessible to
persons in wheelchairs. This compl ies with the Americans With
Disabi lities Act (3); it also provides safety in the event of an
emergency if the only person who can help is in a wheelchair . The
phone should be close to the entrance of the facility or near the
office area so that a supervisor can call emergency services as soon
as possible following an accident (2). The ADA also requires that
light switches, water founta ins, fire extinguishers, and automated
external defibri llator (AED) devices are reachable and that safety
signage is view able b y persons in wheelchairs (3).
A weigh t room should also have double or garage doors so that
large equipment and machine s can be moved in and out. Howev er, if
the hallway or access point is too narrow, the size of the doors will
not make a difference (14), because an outside wall will have to be
removed tempor arily when mo ving or instal ling equipment.
Ceiling  Height
The ceiling should be high enough to accommodate any jumping or
explosiv e activities. This includes the height of the athlete plus the
space required for box jumps, vertical jumps, and weightl ifting
exercises. A recommended height is 12 to 14 feet (3.7-4.3 m), which
should give enough clearance to allow athletes to comfortably
perform most activities (14, 18). Certain exercises, such as overhead
medicine ball throws, may require additional overhead clearance. In
scenarios when sufficient overhead clearance is not available to
perform an exercise, using a nearb y outdoor field, fieldhouse, or
gymnasium should yield enough space. Howev er, these decisions
related to session management should be initiated in the planning
and design process to account for the impact on additional traffic
flow in and out of the f acility during tr aining sessions.

Flooring
Several types of flooring options are available for a strength and
condi tioning facility. The most common option is some type of
rubber flooring  and an antimicr obial carpet. Another option is indoor
turf, which can be useful if the strength and conditioning
professional plans to have athletes perform dynamic warm-up ,
plyometric, agility, or condi tioning exercises. Turf is also a good
surface for ground-based movements and sled pushes that require a
more field-like surface than typical gym flooring. Rubber  flooring is
available in rolls, tile inserts,  and mats and is consider ed a premier
option because it protects the subfloor from heavy weights and
provides durable cushioning. Rubber flooring can also be easier to
clean than carpet and is generally more durable and long-lasting
within a facility. As a gener al rule for safety, floor surfaces in physical
activity areas should be slip-resistant to minimiz e the risk of exercise
and fall-related injuries (2).
For facilities designed to train athletes for weightlifting
competi tions, platforms should have a middle portion made of wood
and outer sections made of rubber , in alignment with competi tion
rules. Especial ly in high-tr affic strength and condi tioning facilities,
inlaid platforms are a safer alternativ e to raised platforms because
they ar e flush wi th the floor and do not pr esent a tripping hazar d.
Lighting
Strength and condi tioning facilities have special lighting needs to
keep athletes safe and focused on training. Key lighting
consider ations should include energy efficiency and the use of
natural lighting when possible (11). To determine proper lighting,
one must consider the squar e footage of the facility, ceiling height,
and the amount of natural sunlight entering the facility through
windows. Lighting is recommended between 20 to 30 foot candles
(fc; 1 fc = lumen/ft2) or 200 to 300 lux (lx; 1 lx = lumen/m2) at floor
level for gener al gym areas and up to 50 foot candles or 500 lux for
dedicated r esistance tr aining and gr oup ex ercise ar eas (2, 11, 18).

The distributio n of light throughout the facility is important to
avoid shadowy areas or excessiv e glare. Glare from direct overhead
lighting is especial ly a concern for users lying on horizontal benches
or exercise mats (18). Windo ws can help make a facility feel more
open and modern, and mirrors may enhance the amount of light
that comes in by reflecting it to spots in the room without natural
light (2). Light-emitting diode (LED) lights are a popular choice
because of their extended life span and energy efficiency (2). Lights
with color temper atures between 4,000° and 5,000° Kelvin are
consider ed best for strength and condi tioning facilities because they
resemble daylight, provide optimal visibility for enhanced safety, and
can have an energizing effect. Finally, adjustable lighting controls,
automated on-and-of f settings, and motion sensors also can be
consider ed to reduce power consumption, energy costs, and
maintenance (11).
Room Temperature, Humidity, and
Ventilation
A strength and condi tioning facility needs to maintain a comfortable
training tempe rature. The heating, ventilation, and air conditioning
(HVAC) system should have the capabi lity to heat and cool individual
sections of the facility. Most sources indicate that between  68 °F and
78 °F (20-26 °C) is an appropriate temper ature range, with many
suggesting an optimal range of 68 °F to 74 °F (20-23 °C) (2, 5, 18).
If the facility is too hot or too cold, the athletes and staff may be
uncomf ortable and the qual ity of tr aining wi ll be af fected.
Relative humidi ty should also be monitored in the strength and
condi tioning facility (6). In any space where there is physical activity,
relative humid ity should not exceed 60%. This helps to prevent
bacterial and microbial growth, avoiding the spread of infections and
disease (2, 15). In addition, areas in a facility with excessiv e
moistur e can contribute to rust and damage weight room equipment
(5). This may be more of a concern if the weight training facility is

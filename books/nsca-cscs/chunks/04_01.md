The endocrine system interacts with the nervous, immune, and
musculosk eletal systems to create a network vital for health and
athletic performance. Hormones regulate cellular activities, including

design training programs that enhance performance, recovery, and
injury prevention. This knowledge allows for manipulating  hormonal

Historical Perspective on Interest in the
Endocrine System
The significance of the endocrine system in strength and
condi tioning gained prominence with the rise of anabol ic drug use in
the 1940s and 1950s (7, 58). Fahey and colleagues were the first to
investigate how resistance exercises affected endogenous hormone
concentr ations, specifically testoster one, across different sexes,
ages, and training statuses,  paving the way for ongoing resear ch
into hormonal responses to resistance exercise and training (23). In
1988, a series of reviews were published from the first symposium
on the physiolo gy of resistance exercise and training at the American
College of Sports Medicine ’s annual meeting. As part of that
symposium set of papers, Kraemer (97) overviewed the sparse
number of studies that had been done on the various hormonal
responses to resistance exercise and training. This stimulated a line
of hormonal resear ch that continues today to explor e further the
mechanisms behind these acute responses and training adaptations
(46). Now the body of literature consists of thousands of studies on
this topic.

Endocrine Terminology, Functions, and
Mechanisms
Grasping the effects of resistance exercise on hormonal responses
requires a solid understanding of endocrine system terminology .
While the learning curve is significant, becoming familiar with this
language is vital for effectively translating scienti fic insights into
practical training strategies. Hormones , the chemical  messengers
or signal molecules, are synthesized, often stored, and released into
the blood by endocrine glands  and certain other cells (figure 4.1,
table 4.1). Neurons also synthesiz e, store, and secrete
neurotransmi tters, which may perform hormonal function s, a study
known as neuroendocrinology —exploring interactions between
the nervous and endocrine systems. Typical ly, endocrine glands are
prompted to release hormones by a chemical signal received by
receptors on the gland or by direct neural stimulation. For example,
the adrenal medul la, the inner part of the adrenal gland, releases
the hormone epinephrine in response to sympathe tic neural
stimulation from the brain (68, 74, 87, 129). Mean while, the outer
layer of the adrenal cortex produces and releases cortisol when
stimulated by adrenocorticotr opic hormone (ACTH) from the pituitary
gland (75, 78, 90). Once released, these hormones travel in the
bloodstr eam to specific receptors located on the surface (for peptide
hormones) or inside the target tissue  cells (for steroid  and
thyroid horm ones ) (4, 5, 6, 8, 22, 39), facilitating a targeted and
efficient r esponse.

Understanding Signaling Concepts
Different types of workouts trigger specific, comple x cellular
endocrine signal ing processes, thus highlighting the importance of
the selection for each of the five acute program variables and then
the total amount of work performed in a workout (70) for the
endocrine response. Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) primari ly directs
protein synthesis, but the regulation and expression of DNA affect
many other cellular functions  and the synthesis of various  molecules
essential for life. Hormonal signal ing processes begin when glands
release hormon es during, or in anticipation of, a resistance exercise
workout. These hormones then bind to specific receptors in muscle
cells and other cells. This binding initiates a cascade of responses,
gener ally resulting in altered cellular metabol ism or DNA
transcription modulation, which can affect protein synthesis. It is
essential to know that every cell and many subcel lular organel les
have genomic and non-geno mic receptors, and this is where the
hormone signals its message to make proteins. Here is a simplified
breakdown of the signal ing pr ocess f or skeletal muscle:
1. Stimulating the muscle . Resistance exercise activates muscles,
prompting a hormonal r esponse.
2. Hormonal response . Hormones are released and travel to
muscle cel ls.
3. Hormone–r eceptor interaction. Horm ones bind to specific
receptors in the cell membr ane of muscle cells (peptide
hormones) or inside the muscle cells (steroid hormones),
signal ing the need f or action inside the cel l.
4. Intracellular signal ing. This binding triggers a series of events
that lead to DNA signal ing inside the nucleus.
5. DNA translation . DNA instructions are transcribed to
messenger ribonucleic acid (mRNA), which carries the
instructions to the cel l’s protein-making machinery (ribosome).

6. Protein production . The mRNA instructions are translated into
specific proteins, the building blocks for muscle repair and
growth.
7. Muscle growth and repair. Regular training uses this muscle
development and repair mechanism, which is essentia l for
developing lean muscle mass and athletic perf ormance.

Functions of the Endocrine System in
Resistance Exercise and Training
The major functions of the endocrine system can be classified into
the following k ey hormonal actions:
Anabol ic responses (to build up). Hormones such as
testoster one, insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), and growth
hormone pla y critical r oles in muscle gr owth and r epair.
Permissiv e actions (to allow). Hormones such as insulin and
thyroid hormo nes enable critical metabol ic and physiolo gical
activities by enhancing nutrient uptak e and promoting
essential chem ical reactions  in muscles, thereby supporting
muscle gr owth and o verall metabol ic functions.
Catabol ic processes (to break down).  For example, cortisol
helps manage energy during stress but can hinder muscle
growth if not properly managed through adequate rest and
recovery. This emphasiz es the balance needed in training
regimens.
Types of Hormonal Release Mechanisms
Hormones are typical ly released into the bloodstr eam via the classic
endocrine pathway after gland stimulation. Hormones  can also
operate through intracrine, autocrine, and paracrine mechanisms,
which do not rely on the bloodstr eam. Intracrine and autocrine
actions involve hormones either binding to internal receptors within
their producing cell or being released to bind to surface receptors on
the same cell. An example of this is insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-
1) and its splice variant mechano growth factor (MGF), which muscle
fibers produce in response to mechanical stress or interaction with
growth hormones, affecting the muscle cell directly. Paracrine action
occurs when hormones affect adjacent cells, again bypassing the
bloodstr eam.

Binding Proteins
Hormones are often transported in the blood by various binding
proteins (4, 6), which serve as a tempor ary storage location and
protect the hormones from degradation, thereby exten ding their
lifetime in the body (called the half-life ). It is important to note that
hormones typically exert their effects only when they are released,
and thus are “free,” from these proteins. Howev er, some binding
proteins, such as sex hormone–binding globul in (SHBG), which binds
testoster one and estrogen, can initiate cellular responses themselv es
by activating pathways, including the cyclic adenosine
monophosphate (cAMP) pathway (29). These interactions are crucial
for understanding endocrine regulation and continue as an area of
study (101).
Target Cells and Tissues
Hormones influence a wide array of cells by interacting with
receptors throughout the body (1, 2, 57, 59, 62, 63). While our focus
is on skeletal  muscle tissue, it is important to recogniz e that
resistance exercise–induced hormone release also affects bone,
other connectiv e tissues, the kidneys, the liver, immune cells, and
neural tissues,  including the brain. Interestingly , not only do these
target cells in the different tissues adapt to the exercise stress, but
their r eceptors also gain a posi tive adaptiv e effect wi th training.

Importance of Recruitment of Muscle
Tissue
Henneman’ s size principle shows that as the resistance increases
(i.e., heavier loads), more and larger muscle fibers are recruited to
meet the increased force demand. This principle outlines the
recruitment order of motor units—from smaller, slow-twitch fiber
motor units to larger, fast-twitch fiber motor units. The heavier the
resistance, the greater the number and size of motor units recruited
in a muscle to produce force (77). While anabol ic and catabol ic
hormones maintain homeostatic balance by interacting with all cells,
stress causes hormonal chang es that reflect the body’s need to meet
new metabol ic demands and recovery needs. Howev er, only
recruited motor units adapt to the training stimulus and are affected
by the endocri ne system’ s increase in anabol ic hormones , likely due
to the increased activation of more receptors. Interestingly, and of
importance, increased catab olic hormone responses to stress or
distress can affect all tissues even if not recruited for exercise
performance. A catab olic state is highly detrimental for an athlete,
especial ly when cortisol levels remain chronical ly elevated. Combined
with the presence of reactiv e oxygen species (free radicals)
gener ated by the body’s inflammatory response, this condi tion
significantly impairs r ecovery and hampers perf ormance (77).

Muscle as the Target for Hormone
Interactions
Resistance exercise and training leads to muscle remodel ing through
a comp lex network of metabol ic and cellular signals. This process
involves muscle fiber damage, an inflammatory response, and the
breakdown of damaged proteins, ultimately synthesizing new
proteins integr ated into muscle structur es. The inflammatory phase,
which involves the immune  system, including T and B cells, is
significantly influenced by hormonal activity, emphasizing the role of
neuroendocrine immunolo gy in understanding how the nervous,
endocrine, and immune systems inter act in muscle adaptation (77).
Hormones regulate the balance between protein synthesis and
degradation, playing a critical role in muscle adaptation after
resistance exercise. They stimulate the production of contractile
proteins like actin and myosin, as well as structur al proteins such as
titin, which are essential for muscle function. Anabolic hormones ,
including insulin, insulin-like growth factors (IGFs), testoster one, and
GH, are particularly vital because they drive tissue growth and
counter act the effects of catabolic hormones  like cortisol and
progester one, which can lead to protein degradation. Furthermor e,
hormones like thyroid hormones play a supportiv e, permissiv e role,
facilitating the actions of other hormones. The potential ly negativ e
effects of cortisol in recovery, such as inhibi ting immune cell activity
and disrupting pathways like the Akt/mechanistic target of
rapam ycin (mTOR) pathway—important for initiating mRNA
translation— are also notable, demonstr ating the complex
interactions between hormones and muscle fibers that influence
muscle f unction and adaptation to tr aining.

Role of Receptors in Me diating
Hormonal Changes
The effectiveness of hormonal signals depends entirely on the
presence of hormone-speci fic receptors on or in the target cells. This
ensur es that each hormone only affects its intended cells and tissues
(those that express the specific receptors). Such specificity is crucial
for maintaining precise hormonal regulation within the body. Since
many hormones cannot directly cross the cell membr ane, they rely
on binding to membr ane receptors that cause secondary
messengers  to transmi t signals internal ly, including reaching the
DNA in the cell’s nuclei. These receptors, located either in the cell
membr ane for polypeptide hormones  or within the cytosol for
steroid and thyroid hormones, allow various cells, from muscle fibers
to immune and br ain cel ls, to r espond to hormonal signals.
A fundamental principle in endocrinology , often described through
the lock-and-key model  (24), explains  how a hormo ne (the key)
must precisely match a receptor (the lock) to trigger a response
(figure 4.2). Howev er, this model oversimpl ifies the complexi ty of
hormone–r eceptor interactions. For example, cross-reactivity
allows recepto rs to engage with hormones other than their primary
matches, and allosteric binding  can modify how a receptor
responds to its primary hormone (such as blocking binding to the
receptor and thus signal ing). Additional ly, receptors might have
allosteric sites where nonhormonal substances can enhance or
reduce the hormonal respons e. Some hormones, like GH, might even
need to aggregate into specific forms (multiple hormones bound
together in a “cluster ”) to effectively signal through their receptors,
as shown in studies using the tibial line assay (40), indicating that
specific molecular configur ations ar e essential f or their activi ty.

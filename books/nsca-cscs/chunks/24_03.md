Underperformance and Its Relation to
Overtraining and Overreaching
Despi te the name of this chapter , all discussions regarding
overtraining and overreaching must start with the concept of
underperformance . Although all our efforts may go to creating
training programs to optimiz e performance, achieving excellence can
be very elusiv e. Seldom are all components of the training and
competi tion processes in complete synchr ony. The job of a strength
and condi tionin g professional is to help navigate the athlete through
the many hurdles standing in the way of achieving absolute peak
performance. Although few, if any, may ever attain that level of
performance perfection, the task of the strength and condi tioning
professional is to develop, prescribe, and apply the type of training
program that gives the athlete the best opportuni ty to reach that
goal. Therefore, it is not only necessary to know how to develop the
appropriately challenging training program required to reach this
level of performance but also to understand what is happe ning when
performance is less than expected (i.e., underperf ormance). We will
see that even though overtraining and overreaching can be
contributors to underperf ormance, many other factors can contribute
as well. Many may be beyond our control, but recognizing possible
contributing factors will permi t the strength and condi tioning
professional to determine what appropriate measur es can be taken
to help the athlete when, despite all the effort, he or she comes up
short. In summary , not all cases of underperf ormance are due to
overtraining or overreaching (figure 24.11). When looking closely at
factors that can adversely influence performance, figure 24.12 lists a
number of factors that can interfere with optimum performance. As
with the stressors that contribute to allostatic load for OTS, many
nontraining v ariables can contribute to underperf ormance.

Performance-Related Variables Affected
by Overtraining and Overreaching
Up to this point we have discussed what contributes to overtraining
and overreaching, but at some point, the strength and condi tioning
professional needs to know how and when performance is affected
(figure 24.14). To better understand performance decrements due to
overtraining, a number of studies have been performed both in the
laboratory and in the field (32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 39, 41, 42, 76, 99,
100, 108, 133). One of the problems with this line of research is the
difficulty in creating an overtraining syndr ome in the laboratory, and
when data are taken from actual athletes who have overtrained, the
lack of study control makes interpr eting the data difficult.
Regardless, table 24.1 lists a number  of performance measur es that
have been shown to be adversely affected by overtraining or are
believed to be vulner able to overtraining (18, 33, 35, 36, 38, 39, 41,
42, 43, 50, 52, 78, 105, 106, 124, 139). The wide range of
performances that can be affected makes understa nding the
practical implications of overtraining challenging. In general, when
overtraining in the weight room, speed (e.g., sprinting) is most
sensitive to overtraining and decreases before most other variables.
This is closely followed by decreases in power (force × velocity),
with maximal strength consistently being one of the last
performances to decrease. It is essential that the strength and
condi tioning professionals monitor training performances to
determine if any of these performances are changing and to include
relevant variables in an athlete moni toring pr ogram.

Physiological Mechanisms Associated
With Overreaching and Overtraining
As discussed earlier, not all forms of overtraining and the resulting
overtraining syndr ome are identical. Furthermor e, the symptoms and
physiological responses can also be quite different depending on the
individual and the characteristics of the training program. Howev er, a
number of physiological systems have either been shown or
theoriz ed to be affected by overtraining. It should be noted that
although these mechanisms can be the result of an overtraining
stimulus, it is also possible that if these condi tions are preexisting,
they can also contribute to an overtraining syndr ome and be
consider ed part of the allostatic load. Understanding the underlying
physiology can, in some cases, provide insight on preventive
strategies to help minimiz e the risk of developing overtraining
syndr ome and the accompan ying impairments in performance. Table
24.2 provides example references for each of the proposed

Assessment of Overreaching and
Overtraining
Ideally, a carefully designed athlete monitoring program is developed
to determine the training and recovery capabi lities of each individual.
Qualities of the tests included  in this program must be specific to the
training being performed or the physiological system involved (i.e.,
valid), reliable (i.e., capable of providing consistent results), sensitive
to the training  and competi tion stimulus, and easy to incorpor ate
into the regular athlete monitoring program. Furthermor e, those
administering the tests must precisely follow correct protocols and
must ensure that each person performing the tests provides a
maximal effort. If the tests are not administer ed correctly, the data
acquir ed will be meaningles s. Once the tests are performed, an
efficient data management system must be used to help best
interpr et the results rapidly and correctly. Rapid turnar ound of test
results is important to permi t adjustments in the training program
when needed.

Physiological Testing
Although the strength and condi tioning professional does not
typical ly order medical or laboratory tests to monitor the health of
his or her athletes and the risk of overtraining, it is helpful to know
what types of tests are available and what information they provide.
Numer ous types of medical and physiological tests have been used
to help combat the onset of overtraining syndr ome. In gener al, the
meaningf ulness of these tests has been equiv ocal, dependent on the
subjects being studied, the training program being performed, and
the sport being monitored. A detailed description of all the tests
possible is beyond the scope of this chapter , but the reader is
welcome to examine some of the following references to get an idea
of what is available (9, 10, 19, 34, 36, 37, 39, 40, 45, 50, 54-57 , 59,
65, 72, 78-81, 84, 88, 89, 98, 104, 113, 123, 124, 126, 131, 138). In
gener al, numer ous potential ly helpful variables can be assessed from
the blood (e.g., hormone s and neurohormones, interleukins,
cytokines, markers of muscle disruption or damage , immune
factors), cardiovascular measur es (e.g., resting heart rate, heart rate
variabi lity, oxygen uptak e, muscle oxygen saturation), skeletal
muscle measur es (e.g., heat shock proteins, cell-signal ing pathways,
receptors), neuromuscular measur es (e.g., motor unit recruitment or
EMG activity), and orthopedic factors (e.g., joint inflammation or
inhibi tory input). Data of this type can help the resear cher and the
practitioner better understand the effects of overtraining and
perhaps eventually provide reliable biomark ers of a developing
overtraining syndr ome.

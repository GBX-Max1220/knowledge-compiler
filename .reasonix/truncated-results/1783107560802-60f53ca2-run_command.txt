HEALTH	BENEFITS	OF	IMPROVING
MUSCULAR	FITNESS
The health benefits of enhancing muscular fitness (
i.e.
, the functional
parameters of muscle strength, endurance, and power) are well
established (
16

cardiometabolic risk profiles, lower risk of all-cause mortality, fewer
CVD events, lower risk of developing physical function limitations, and
lower risk for nonfatal disease (
2

health-related biomarkers can be realized with regular participation in
resistance training, including improvements in body composition, blood
glucose levels, insulin sensitivity, and blood pressure in individuals with
mild or moderate hypertension (
2
, 
88
, 
89
). Recent evidence suggests that
resistance training is as effective as endurance training in the
management and treatment of Type 2 diabetes mellitus (
90
, 
91
) and in
improving the blood lipid profiles of individuals who are overweight or
obese (
92
). Resistance training positively affects walking distance and
velocity in those with peripheral artery disease (
91
, 
93
). Further health
benefits attributed to resistance training are supported by several meta-
analyses indicating that regimens featuring mild- to moderate-intensity
isometric muscle actions are more effective in reducing blood pressure
in both normal people and people with hypertension than aerobic
training or dynamic resistance training alone or in combination (
94
, 
95
).
Accordingly, resistance training may be effective for preventing and
treating conditions related to metabolic syndrome (
2
) (see 
Chapter	9
).

EXERCISE-RELATED	CARDIAC	EVENTS	IN
ADULTS
In general, exercise does not elicit cardiovascular events in healthy
individuals without congenital or inherited cardiovascular
predispositions (
122
). The risk of SCD and AMI is very low in apparently
healthy (
i.e.
, no signs or symptoms of CVD) individuals performing
moderate-intensity PA (
123
, 
124
). There is an acute and transient
increase in the risk of SCD and AMI in individuals performing vigorous-
intensity PA, particularly in sedentary men and women with diagnosed
or occult CVD (
100
, 
125
). However, this risk decreases with long-term
compliance to an exercise regimen and increasing volumes of regular PA
(
100
, 
122
). 
Chapter	2
 includes an exercise preparticipation health
screening algorithm to help identify individuals who may be at risk for
exercise-related cardiovascular events.
It is well established that the transient risks of SCD and AMI are
substantially higher during and immediately (~1 hour) after acute
vigorous PA than rest (
100
, 
103
, 
126
, 
127
). A meta-analysis of published
--- PAGE 94 ---
studies reported a fivefold increased risk of SCD and a 3.5-fold increased
risk of AMI during or shortly after vigorous-intensity PA (
125
). The risk
of SCD or AMI is higher in middle-aged and older adults than in younger
individuals due to the higher prevalence of CVD in the older population.
The rates of SCD and AMI are disproportionately more elevated in the
most inactive individuals when they perform unaccustomed or
infrequent exercise (
100
, 
103

Onset Study (
102

PREVENTION	OF	EXERCISE-RELATED
CARDIAC	EVENTS
Because of the low incidence of cardiac events related to vigorous-
intensity exercise, it is very difficult to test the effectiveness of strategies
to reduce the occurrence of these events. Therefore, in a 2007 joint
report by the ACSM and AHA, the authors suggested that “physicians
should not overestimate the risks of exercise because the benefits of
habitual physical activity substantially outweigh the risks” (
100
). This
report also recommends several strategies to reduce risk of these cardiac
events during vigorous-intensity exercise (
100
):
Health care professionals should know the pathologic conditions
associated with exercise-related events to appropriately evaluate
physically active children and adults.
Physically active individuals should know the nature of cardiac
prodromal symptoms (

chest and/or upper back) and seek prompt medical care if such
symptoms develop (see
